📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why do empires fall apart?

Conquering dozens of independent Sumerian city-states, as Sargon did, is one problem. Governing them afterward, so they stay conquered, is a much harder one. This lesson looks at how the Akkadian Empire tried to solve that problem, and then at the still-debated question of why, roughly a century and a half later, it collapsed.

📚 PART 1 · STANDARDIZING AN EMPIRE

Sargon and his successors, most notably his grandson Naram-Sin, pursued a genuinely new administrative strategy: instead of leaving each conquered city entirely to its own local rulers and customs, they installed Akkadian-appointed governors, promoted the Akkadian language (a Semitic language distinct from Sumerian) alongside Sumerian in official business, and worked to standardize weights, measures, and administrative practice across the empire's many cities. This mattered enormously for the empire's ability to function: a merchant, tax collector, or soldier moving from one city to another could expect roughly the same units, the same expectations, and increasingly the same language of government, wherever they went. Naram-Sin went further than any Akkadian king before him, taking the title "King of the Four Quarters (of the world)" and, according to some traditions, even claiming a kind of divine status for himself during his lifetime, an extraordinary claim for a Mesopotamian king to make.

📚 PART 2 · THE CURSE OF AKKAD'S EXPLANATION

And then, within a few generations, the Akkadian Empire fell apart. A Sumerian literary composition called the Curse of Akkad, which you will read from directly this week, offers a striking explanation: Naram-Sin, in the poem's telling, defied the will of the great god Enlil and destroyed Enlil's own temple, the Ekur at Nippur. In response, Enlil cursed the city of Akkad itself, bringing famine, and calling down a fierce nomadic people, the Gutians, to overrun the land. The poem reads Akkad's collapse as a moral and religious event: hubris, a king's overreaching pride, invites divine punishment, and a civilization that abandons proper reverence toward the gods loses its right to endure.

⚠️ DON'T PICK JUST ONE CAUSE
This is worth taking seriously on its own terms rather than dismissing outright, because it reflects something genuinely important about how the people who lived through Akkad's collapse tried to make sense of it: they looked for a moral cause behind a political disaster, not merely a material one. At the same time, modern historians point to other, more material contributing causes: study of ancient soil and pollen layers suggests a serious, extended drought struck the region around the time of Akkad's fall, straining the irrigation-dependent agriculture Week 2 already told you Sumer depended on. Gutian incursions from the Zagros Mountains, mentioned in the poem itself, were also very likely real historical events, whatever their supernatural framing.
📚 PART 3 · WEIGHING AN ANCIENT EXPLANATION AGAINST A MODERN ONE

Which explanation is correct? This is exactly the kind of question this course wants you practicing: not "which single cause is the whole answer," but "how do a civilization's own moral explanation and a modern historian's material explanation each capture something real, without either one being the complete picture?" A drought does not, by itself, force a king to defy the priesthood of Enlil, and a moral failing does not, by itself, dry up a river. Both threads were very likely operating together.

Notice, too, that Akkad's fall does not fit a story of steady progress. Great empires can decline and dissolve, not simply grow ever stronger, and the reasons why are rarely simple. Keep this lesson in mind: the Bronze Age Collapse, which closes this quarter in Week 9, will ask this same question again, at a much larger scale.
