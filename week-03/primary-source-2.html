💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Excerpted and lightly adapted from public-domain and openly published scholarly renderings of the Curse of Akkad, a Sumerian literary composition mourning the fall of the Akkadian Empire, most likely composed generations after the empire's actual collapse.
📖 THE TEXT

For the first time since cities were built and founded, the great agricultural tracts produced no grain, the flooded tracts produced no fish, the watered gardens produced neither wine nor syrup, the thick clouds did not rain, the mash-grain did not grow. At that time, one shekel's worth of oil was only half a quart, one shekel's worth of grain was only half a quart. These were sold at such prices in the markets of all the cities. Whoever slept on the roof, died on the roof. Whoever slept in the house, had no burial. People were flailing at themselves from hunger.

All this happened because Naram-Sin, king of Akkad, in his pride and in defiance of the word of the god Enlil, had gone up against the Ekur, Enlil's own temple at Nippur, and had torn it down as a mountain is torn down, had reduced the temple to rubble as a great ship is broken apart. For this, the great gods Enlil, Anu, Enki, and Ninhursaga cursed Akkad with a great curse.

Enlil brought down from the mountains those who do not resemble other people, who are not reckoned as part of the land, the Gutian people, an unbridled people, with human intelligence but canine instincts and monkeys' features. Like small birds they swooped on the ground in great flocks. Because of Enlil, nothing escaped their grasp, no one escaped their clutches. Messengers no longer traveled the highways, the courier's boat no longer went along the rivers. The Gutians drove the trusted goats of Enlil from their folds and made their herdsmen follow them. They drove the cows from their pens and made their cowherds follow them. Prisoners guarded prisoners, and captives guarded captives.

The regular offerings of the gods' temples were suspended. The great rulers of Akkad no longer secured the corn deliveries. The wide streets, where celebrations were once held, were overgrown with weeds. All the joyous places of the land became evil places.

Then Enlil, whose city had been destroyed, gathered a curse in his mouth: "The one who destroyed my city, let the destruction fall upon his own city. The one who destroyed the Ekur, let the destruction fall upon the Ekur that is his own. Akkad, may your canal boats no longer travel upon the canals. May your city, which was so well founded, become like a ruin mound. May your people, who ate splendid food, lie down in the grass, may your people, who drank pure water, drink stagnant water."
