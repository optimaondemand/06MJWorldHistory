📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What if the basket had sunk?

Every civilization studied so far in this course, Jericho, Çatalhöyük, Uruk, has been a single city or a single small region. This week that changes. Around 2334 BC, a man named Sargon built something the world had never seen: an empire, a single political power ruling over many previously separate cities and peoples at once, stretching across all of Mesopotamia and beyond. To understand how strange and unlikely this achievement was, you have to start with the story Sargon himself, or scribes writing in his name generations later, told about his own birth.

📚 PART 1 · AN IMPROBABLE BEGINNING

According to the Sargon Birth Legend, a text you will read directly this week, Sargon was born to a priestess who could not openly raise him. She placed her infant son in a basket sealed with tar and set him adrift on the Euphrates River. The basket floated downstream until a man named Akki, a drawer of water, discovered it, rescued the child, and raised him as his own son, eventually training him as a gardener. From this humble, obscure beginning, the story says, the goddess Ishtar came to favor young Sargon, and he rose to become cupbearer, a position of real trust and access, to the king of the city of Kish. From cupbearer, Sargon eventually seized power for himself and began the conquests that built the Akkadian Empire.

Whether every detail of this story happened exactly as told is impossible to verify, and that uncertainty is itself worth sitting with rather than resolving too quickly. What matters for how you think about history is this: the entire empire that follows, the standardized administration, the conquered cities, the common language spreading across Mesopotamia, rests on a chain of events that did not have to happen. If the basket had sunk, if Akki had not been drawing water at that exact bend of the river that day, if Sargon had never caught the king's attention, none of the rest of this lesson would exist to tell. This is what historians mean by contingency: the real past was not marching toward Sargon's empire as an inevitable destination. A single improbable childhood, real or legendary, stands at the root of the first empire in recorded history.

📚 PART 2 · FROM CUPBEARER TO CONQUEROR

Once in power, Sargon moved with real speed. He conquered the Sumerian city-states one by one, including Uruk, the city you studied last week, defeating its king Lugalzagesi and bringing the whole of southern Mesopotamia under one rule for the first time. He then pushed his conquests northward and westward, campaigning as far as the Mediterranean coast and into parts of Anatolia and Elam, in what is now western Iran. Sargon founded a new capital city, Akkad, whose exact location has never been rediscovered by archaeologists, a small but telling reminder that even a capital city commanding an empire can vanish from the physical map while surviving in written memory.

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW · WHY "EMPIRE" IS A NEW KIND OF WORD
A kingdom typically rules one people, in one place, under one set of customs. An empire rules many different peoples, cities, and languages at once, peoples who did not necessarily choose to be ruled together and who often kept their own local customs, gods, and elites even while answering to a single distant authority. Holding that kind of arrangement together, across hundreds of miles, with no telephones, no rapid roads, and no professional bureaucracy inherited from anyone else, was a genuinely new political problem.
