📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Could a pharaoh be great without ever going to war?

Egypt's Old Kingdom, the pyramid-building age Week 5 introduced, did not last forever. Around 2181 BC, central authority weakened, regional governors grew more powerful than the pharaoh in Memphis, and Egypt splintered into a period historians call the First Intermediate Period. Egypt reunified under the Middle Kingdom (c. 2055 to 1650 BC), a period of renewed stability, expanded trade, and some of Egypt's finest literature. That stability broke down again in the Second Intermediate Period, when a Semitic-speaking people called the Hyksos took control of Lower Egypt from their capital at Avaris, ruling as a foreign, if partly Egyptianized, dynasty from roughly 1650 to 1550 BC. The Hyksos are remembered for one especially consequential import: the horse-drawn chariot and the composite bow, technologies Egypt would soon turn into the backbone of its own New Kingdom armies. Egyptian princes eventually expelled the Hyksos and reunified the country under the New Kingdom (c. 1550 to 1150 BC), the era this week and next week's lessons explore, an age of empire, ambitious pharaohs, and, in this lesson, a queen who ruled as king.

📚 PART 1 · AN UNUSUAL PATH TO THE THRONE

Hatshepsut came to power around 1479 BC, first as regent for her young stepson and nephew Thutmose III, and then, within a few years, as pharaoh in her own right, adopting the full royal titulary, regalia, and even the traditional false beard of Egyptian kingship, though inscriptions and statues sometimes describe or depict her with feminine pronouns and features alongside these masculine royal symbols. This was, by any measure, an unusual path. Egyptian kingship had never formally excluded women, but almost no woman before Hatshepsut had claimed the full throne and held it for roughly two decades, as she did. Her officials worked to justify her rule through careful inscriptions describing her as chosen by the god Amun himself, a divine-selection story not unlike the divine kingship claim Week 5 introduced, now stretched to cover a genuinely unprecedented situation.

📚 PART 2 · TRADE INSTEAD OF CONQUEST

Rather than building her reputation primarily through military campaigns, Hatshepsut is best remembered for a different kind of achievement: a major trading expedition to the land of Punt, likely located along the Red Sea coast of modern Eritrea, Sudan, or Somalia, sent out around the ninth year of her reign. This week's Primary Source lets you read the inscriptions Hatshepsut had carved at her mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari, recording the voyage in vivid detail: ships loaded for the return journey with incense trees dug up with their root balls intact, ebony, ivory, gold, myrrh resin, leopard skins, and even live baboons.

Egypt's own economy could not produce many of these luxury and ritual goods domestically. Incense in particular was burned constantly in temple rituals across Egypt, making a reliable supply from Punt a genuine economic and religious necessity, not merely a display of royal wealth. This is a useful case study in scarcity: Egypt lacked certain resources it needed, and rather than simply doing without them or seizing them by force, Hatshepsut's government organized a large, carefully planned trading and diplomatic expedition to acquire them through exchange.

💡 A DIFFERENT KIND OF GREATNESS
Compare Hatshepsut's approach to what you might expect from an ambitious New Kingdom pharaoh eager to prove himself through war. Hatshepsut's Punt expedition, and the peaceful building projects that filled much of her reign, represent a genuine, deliberate choice: greatness achieved through prudent administration, careful diplomacy, and successful trade, rather than through the battlefield. Whether this made her a lesser pharaoh than a conquering warrior-king is a real question worth weighing, not a question with an obvious answer already settled. Wednesday's lesson introduces her stepson, Thutmose III, who chose a strikingly different path to greatness once the throne became fully his own, and this week's assessments, including your VR activity, will ask you to judge the two approaches side by side.
