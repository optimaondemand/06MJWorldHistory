📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What if Thutmose III had listened to his own officers?

When Hatshepsut died around 1458 BC, her stepson Thutmose III finally became sole ruler of Egypt in more than name, after years as a co-regent standing in his stepmother's shadow. Where Hatshepsut had built her legacy on trade and diplomacy, Thutmose III built his on the battlefield, and would go on to lead as many as seventeen military campaigns into the Levant over his reign, earning him the nickname later historians gave him: the "Napoleon of Egypt." His first campaign, launched almost immediately after taking full power, produced the earliest battle in world history for which a detailed, specific account survives.

📚 PART 1 · A REBELLION IN CANAAN

A coalition of Canaanite and Syrian rulers, backed by the powerful kingdom of Mitanni to the northeast, had gathered their combined forces at the fortified city of Megiddo, in modern northern Israel, in open rebellion against Egyptian authority. Thutmose III marched his army north to confront them. This week's Primary Source, drawn from the official Annals of Thutmose III recorded on temple walls at Karnak, describes what happened next in unusual detail for an ancient military record.

📚 PART 2 · A RISKY CHOICE OF ROADS

Three roads led to Megiddo through the surrounding hills: two wider, safer routes approaching from the sides, and one narrow, direct mountain pass so tight that soldiers would have to march single file, horse following horse, completely exposed if the enemy chose to attack while the Egyptian army was strung out along it. According to the Annals, Thutmose III's own war council of officers argued strongly against the narrow pass, urging him to take one of the safer roads instead. Thutmose III overruled them, reasoning that the enemy would expect an Egyptian approach from the safer routes and would concentrate their own forces there, leaving the direct pass undefended. He personally led his army through the narrow pass at its head, sharing the same risk he was asking his soldiers to accept.

The gamble paid off. The Egyptian army emerged from the pass completely unopposed, exactly as Thutmose III had predicted, and was able to form up in the open plain before Megiddo while the coalition forces were still positioned elsewhere. In the battle that followed, the coalition army broke and fled back into the city, and Megiddo, after the field itself was already Thutmose III's, held out under siege for seven more months before finally surrendering.

⚠️ CONTINGENCY AT THE MOUNTAIN PASS
This is worth pausing on as a genuine historical contingency, in the spirit of Sargon's basket and Hammurabi's late-life conquests from earlier weeks. Thutmose III's own officers thought the narrow pass was reckless, and by ordinary military logic, they had a point: had the coalition army simply posted a modest defensive force at the pass's far end, the exposed, single-file Egyptian column could have been badly mauled before ever reaching open ground. Nothing about this outcome was guaranteed in advance. A different set of scouts, a slightly different coalition strategy, or a pharaoh less willing to overrule his own generals, and Egyptian history, and this course's account of it, might record a very different first campaign for Thutmose III.
📚 PART 3 · TWO PHARAOHS, TWO VIRTUES

Set beside Hatshepsut's Punt expedition from Monday's lesson, Thutmose III's Megiddo campaign offers a deliberate contrast in what ancient greatness could look like. Hatshepsut pursued prudence, patience, and successful diplomacy; Thutmose III pursued a bold, personally led gamble that expanded Egyptian power by force. Neither path was obviously the correct one, and both rulers are remembered, in their own ways, as successful pharaohs. This week's assessments, including your VR activity, will ask you to weigh both approaches directly against each other.
