💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from early Egyptological translations of the official Annals of Thutmose III, inscribed on the walls of the temple of Amun at Karnak, describing the campaign against the rebel coalition at Megiddo, 1457 BC.
📖 THE ANNALS

The war council debates the route:
"Then his officers said before his majesty: 'How can we go upon this road, narrow as it is reported to be, when it is said that the enemy stand waiting outside it, and they are many? Will not horse have to follow behind horse, and man behind man, in the same way? Shall our advance-guard be fighting while our rear-guard stands still, not yet having reached the pass at all?'"

Thutmose III overrules them:
"His majesty swore an oath, saying: 'As Re loves me, as my father Amun favors me, my majesty will proceed upon this narrow road of Aruna. Let whoever among you wishes go upon those other roads you have described, and let whoever wishes come in the following of my majesty.' Then they said: 'May your father Amun protect you. Behold, we are the followers of your majesty in every place you go, for the servant follows after his lord.'"

The Egyptian army passes through the pass unopposed:
"Then his majesty went forth at the head of his own army, himself showing the way by his own footsteps, horse following behind horse, his majesty being at the head of his troops. Not one among the enemy came out of the city to fight, for the fear of his majesty had entered into their bodies, and they had gathered instead upon the other roads, exactly as his majesty had judged they would."

A moment of frustration, after the enemy coalition breaks and flees:
"Now if only the army of his majesty had not given their hearts over to plundering the possessions of the enemy, they would have captured Megiddo at that very moment, for the wretched enemy of Kadesh and the wretched enemy of Megiddo were at that moment being hauled up hastily over the wall of their city to bring them inside it, so great was the fear of his majesty that had entered their bodies. Their horses and their chariots of gold and silver were left abandoned and captured instead of the men themselves."

A concluding judgment on the victory's significance:
"The capturing of Megiddo is the capturing of a thousand cities."
