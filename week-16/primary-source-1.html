💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Homer's Iliad, Book 1, describing the quarrel that opens the poem.
📖 THE ILIAD, BOOK 1

On the cause of the quarrel:
"Agamemnon, forced by a plague sent from Apollo to give up his own captive woman, seized Briseis, the captive of Achilles, in her place, to satisfy his wounded pride. Achilles, greatest of the Greek warriors at Troy, burned with anger at the insult and swore before the assembled army that he would fight no longer."

On Achilles's decision to withdraw:
"'I will not lift my spear for Agamemnon's sake, nor for the Greeks he commands, since he has dishonored the best man among them. Let the Trojans and Hector's spear drive the Greeks back to their ships. Then perhaps Agamemnon will know what he has lost by disrespecting me.'"

On the poem's own framing of the cost:
"Sing, goddess, of the wrath of Achilles, son of Peleus, that ruinous wrath which brought countless sorrows upon the Greeks, and sent the souls of many brave warriors down to the house of the dead, all because of the quarrel between Agamemnon, lord of men, and godlike Achilles."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
This excerpt is drawn from the very opening of the Iliad, and its first word in the original Greek is "wrath." Homer states outright, before the story even properly begins, that Achilles's anger will cost many lives. This course studies this episode not to determine whether the quarrel was historically real, which cannot be confirmed, but to examine what the poem itself says about pride, honor, and the real damage an otherwise genuinely courageous person can cause when wounded pride goes unchecked.
