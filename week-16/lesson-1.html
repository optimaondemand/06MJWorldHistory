📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What happens to a civilization that loses its own writing system?

This course left Greece in ruins back in Week 9. The great Mycenaean palace centers, Pylos, Mycenae, Tiryns, and the rest, had burned or emptied out during the Bronze Age Collapse, their wanax-led palace economies gone along with them. What follows is one of this course's clearest examples of a civilization not recovering quickly, or in some ways not recovering at all for a very long time.

📚 PART 1 · A GENUINE DECLINE, NOT A PAUSE

Historians call the roughly three and a half centuries that follow, from about 1150 to 800 BC, the Greek Dark Age, and the name is earned rather than exaggerated. Population fell sharply and stayed low. The great palace centers were not merely damaged. They were abandoned outright, never rebuilt on anything like their former scale. Long-distance trade across the Mediterranean, which the Mycenaeans had once conducted routinely, dried up to a trickle. Monumental building, of the kind this course studied at Mycenae's Cyclopean walls in Week 8, simply stopped.

Most striking of all, Linear B, the palace-administrative script this course met in Week 8, vanished entirely. It had never been a widely taught skill in the first place, confined to specially trained scribes working for the palace bureaucracies. When those palaces disappeared, the scribes and their training disappeared with them, and Greece became, in a very real sense, functionally illiterate for roughly the next three centuries. This is worth stating plainly, because it cuts directly against any assumption that history only ever moves forward. A society can lose a skill as fundamental as writing itself, and simply not have it again for generations. That is exactly what happened here.

📚 PART 2 · ONE THREAD THAT DID NOT BREAK

Not everything about this period was pure loss. As the bronze-dependent tin trade collapsed along with the wider Mediterranean trade network this course has followed since Phoenicia and Egypt's Bronze Age contacts, a different metal filled the gap. Ironworking, already given its full spotlight treatment with the Hittites back in Week 8, spread widely across Greece during exactly this dark, impoverished period. Iron ore, unlike tin, could be found locally in many parts of Greece, and did not depend on the kind of long-distance trade network that had just collapsed. A poorer, smaller, less literate Greece nonetheless became, quietly, an increasingly ironworking Greece. Decline and technological adaptation were happening at the very same time, in the very same struggling villages, a genuine complication worth holding onto rather than flattening into a single simple story.

📖 CLOSING · WHAT SURVIVES WHEN THE RECORD DOES NOT

Here is the real puzzle this Dark Age leaves behind. This course has grown used to studying civilizations through their own written words: Hammurabi's Code, Piye's stele, Ashurbanipal's library tablets. Dark Age Greece left behind essentially none of that. Its people were mostly farmers and herders scattered in small villages, with little wealth, no palace bureaucracy, and no writing system in active use at all.

And yet the memory of the vanished Mycenaean world did not vanish along with the palaces. It survived instead in a form this course has not yet studied closely: oral tradition, stories, genealogies, and songs passed by memory from one generation of storytellers to the next, with no written text to anchor them, for three centuries or more before anyone thought to write them down. What such a memory can preserve, and what it inevitably changes, adds, or loses along the way, is exactly the problem Lesson 2 turns to directly, through the two poems that eventually grew out of this very tradition.
