📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can the sky itself end a war?

By the time of King Cyaxares (r. 625 to 585 BC), the Medes were no longer anyone's tribute-paying frontier people. They were one of the two powers that ended the Neo-Assyrian Empire outright, joining Babylon in the siege and destruction of Nineveh in 612 BC, an event this course studied in full in Week 12. In barely more than a century, the Medes had gone from near-invisibility in the historical record to bringing down the largest empire the Near East had yet seen.

📚 PART 1 · A WAR THAT ENDED WITHOUT A WINNER

Cyaxares's Median kingdom soon found itself at war with another rising power to the west, Lydia, a wealthy kingdom in Anatolia under King Alyattes. This week's second Primary Source, again drawn from Herodotus, describes a war that dragged on for roughly five years with no decisive victory for either side, until, during a battle in its sixth year, something extraordinary happened: the day suddenly turned to night. A solar eclipse, which Herodotus reports had been predicted in advance by the Greek thinker Thales of Miletus, interrupted the fighting so completely that both armies stopped at once, apparently unnerved by an omen neither side wanted to defy. Both sides quickly pursued peace, formalized through a diplomatic marriage between the two royal families and a new, mutually accepted border along the Halys River in Anatolia.

📚 PART 2 · DEEP-DIVE SPOTLIGHT: ANCIENT ASTRONOMY

This episode deserves this course's full attention as a deep-dive spotlight on astronomy, distinct from the mathematics spotlight this course gave Babylon back in Week 4. Mesopotamian and Near Eastern observers had spent centuries carefully tracking the sky, recording the regular movements of the sun, moon, and stars for religious, agricultural, and administrative purposes, and this patient observation had, by the sixth century BC, developed real predictive power. Whether or not Thales specifically deserves credit for this particular prediction, a question ancient and modern scholars have both debated, the underlying point stands: a natural event that would have seemed like nothing but a terrifying omen to any observer without this tradition of careful sky-watching instead became something at least some contemporaries could reportedly see coming. Prediction, even imperfect prediction, is a genuinely different relationship with the natural world than superstition alone, though this course should be careful not to overstate: fear of the eclipse, not calm scientific confidence, is still what actually stopped the battle.

⚠️ SLOWLY, THEN ALL AT ONCE, ONE FINAL TIME
Consider the full arc this week has traced: four centuries of near-total obscurity, a legendary founding king whose story this course cannot fully verify, and then, within a single lifetime, an empire strong enough to help destroy Assyria and negotiate as an equal with a wealthy Anatolian kingdom. This is the same pattern this course keeps returning to, because it keeps genuinely happening: history does not move at a steady pace, and long periods of apparent stillness can give way, with startling speed, to consequential change.
📖 CLOSING

One final piece of this pattern deserves to be named directly, since it closes this week's story with real irony. The same Median royal house that had grown so powerful, and negotiated so successfully with Lydia, was not destined to remain in power much longer. Within a few decades, Cyaxares's own line would be overthrown, not by a foreign invader, but by his own grandson, a young Persian vassal king this course will meet directly, and in full, in Week 17.
