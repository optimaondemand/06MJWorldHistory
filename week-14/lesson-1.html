📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What does it mean for a people to be building toward something long before history notices?

Every civilization this course has studied so far has left behind a fairly steady written record of its own rise. This week's subject, the Medes, are different, and that difference is itself worth understanding before their story can be told at all.

📚 PART 1 · A PEOPLE ALMOST INVISIBLE IN THE RECORD

The Medes were an Iranian-speaking people who settled in and around the Zagros Mountains, the rugged highland region east of Mesopotamia, sometime after the Bronze Age Collapse this course studied in Week 9. For roughly the next four hundred years, from about 1150 to 728 BC, the Medes appear in the historical record only faintly and occasionally, mostly as one tribute-paying people among many along Assyria's eastern frontier, the same kind of relationship this course studied directly through Jehu of Israel in Week 11. No Median king from this long stretch left behind inscriptions of his own the way Assyrian, Babylonian, or Egyptian kings did. This course simply does not have a Median version of Ashurnasirpal's annals or Piye's stele for these centuries, and it is important to be honest about that gap rather than pretend otherwise.

This kind of near-invisibility is worth pausing on. It does not mean nothing was happening among the Medes during these four centuries. Communities were forming, tribal and family structures were organizing local life, and, gradually, something like shared political identity was taking shape, all of it simply outside the notice, or at least outside the surviving written record, of the literate empires nearby. A people can be building toward something significant for a very long time before that process becomes visible to history at all.

📚 PART 2 · DEIOCES, ACCORDING TO HERODOTUS

This week's first Primary Source turns to the Greek historian Herodotus, already introduced in Week 10, for the fullest surviving account of how the Medes are said to have first unified under a single ruler. Herodotus tells a genuinely memorable story: a Median villager named Deioces, known locally for his fairness in settling disputes, became so trusted that people from beyond his own village began bringing him their conflicts to judge. According to Herodotus, Deioces then deliberately withdrew from judging altogether, and disorder among the Medes grew worse without him, until the Medes themselves gathered and chose him as their first king. Once in power, Deioces is said to have built an elaborate, carefully guarded court at a new capital, Ecbatana, with concentric walls of different colors, and to have surrounded himself with strict ceremony, forbidding even laughter in his presence, specifically to keep the nobles who had recently been his equals from feeling too familiar with him to plot against him.

This is a wonderful story, and it needs to be read the way Week 10 taught this course to read Herodotus generally. It may preserve a real memory of how Median kingship actually began, built from below through a genuine reputation for justice rather than conquest, a notably different origin story than most kingdoms this course has studied. It may also be, in whole or in part, a legendary account, shaped over generations of retelling before Herodotus ever wrote it down, the same kind of caution this course applied to Sargon's basket in Week 3 or Gilgamesh's flood in Week 2. Modern historians remain genuinely divided on how much of Deioces's story reflects real history. The honest answer, once again, is that this course simply cannot be certain, and pretending otherwise would be worse than admitting the uncertainty directly.

📖 CLOSING

Whatever its origins, a unified Median kingdom did eventually exist, and its rise from a barely visible frontier people to a major regional power is one of this course's clearest examples yet of a pattern worth naming directly: slowly, then all at once. Lesson 2 picks up exactly there.
