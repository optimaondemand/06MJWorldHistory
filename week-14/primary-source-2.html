Herodotus's Account of the Battle of the Eclipse (585 BC)
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Herodotus's Histories, Book I, describing the war between Media and Lydia and the eclipse that helped end it.
📖 THE HISTORIES, BOOK I

On the course of the war before the eclipse:
"War continued between the Lydians and the Medes for five years, during which each side won victories over the other in turn, without either gaining a lasting advantage. In the sixth year, a battle took place in which the two armies were still engaged, evenly matched, when suddenly day became night in the midst of the fighting."

On the prediction of this event:
"This change of day into night had been foretold to the Greeks by Thales of Miletus, who had set the very year in which it occurred as the limit for his prediction, though he could not say the precise day."

On the armies' reaction:
"When the Lydians and the Medes saw this happening, they ceased their fighting at once, and both sides seemed far more eager to make peace with one another than they had been before."

On the resulting peace settlement:
"The peace between them was arranged with the help of other rulers acting as go-betweens, and was sealed by a marriage between the families of the two kings. They agreed that the river Halys should form the boundary between their two kingdoms, and both sides swore to keep faith with this agreement."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Modern astronomers, working backward from known eclipse patterns, generally agree a solar eclipse visible in this region did occur around 585 BC, lending some real support to this account, though historians still debate exactly how much credit Thales personally deserves for predicting it in advance.
