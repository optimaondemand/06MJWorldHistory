📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What does it take to read an outsider's account of a civilization well?

Nearly everything this course has read so far comes from the civilizations themselves: royal inscriptions, law codes, king lists, administrative tablets. This week's second Primary Source is different. It comes from an outsider, a Greek traveler writing roughly two centuries after Piye, who set out to describe Egypt for readers who had never seen it.

📚 PART 1 · MEET HERODOTUS

Herodotus, born in the Greek city of Halicarnassus in the fifth century BC, is often called the "Father of History," not because he was the first person ever to write about the past, but because he was the first to approach it the way this course tries to: traveling to see things for himself, questioning multiple sources, and openly telling his readers when he was uncertain or skeptical rather than simply repeating whatever he had been told as settled fact. His book, the Histories, devotes an entire section, Book II, to Egypt, and this week's Primary Source draws from it directly.

Herodotus deserves careful reading, not uncritical trust. He genuinely traveled and genuinely questioned Egyptian priests and other informants, and much of what he reports about Egyptian customs, religious practice, and the Nile's flooding holds up well against what archaeology and Egyptian sources themselves confirm. But he was also a foreigner working through translators, centuries removed from the events he sometimes describes, and willing to pass along stories he found remarkable even when he openly doubted them himself. Reading him well means learning to tell these two kinds of material apart, which is exactly this week's Critical Questions and Primary Source Exercise ask you to practice.

📚 PART 2 · TWO KINDS OF CLAIM

Consider two examples from his own text. Herodotus reports, carefully and at some length, several competing theories for why the Nile floods every year on a predictable schedule, weighs each one, and offers his own reasoned judgment, a genuinely historical way of handling a real puzzle. But he also reports a story about a phoenix bird that supposedly carries its dead father's body, wrapped in myrrh, from Arabia to the temple of the sun once every five hundred years, a story he explicitly tells his readers he does not fully believe, only that this is what the Egyptians say. That single word, "say," carried honestly through a text, is itself a piece of historical method worth noticing. Herodotus is not lying to us. He is showing his work.

🎯 TRY IT: READING HERODOTUS
Sort two of Herodotus's own claims by the kind of evidence behind them.
"Having weighed what I have seen and heard, my own opinion is that the flood results from the sun's changing position through the seasons."
"It is said to carry its father's body, wrapped in myrrh, all the way from Arabia to the temple of the sun. I do not vouch for its truth."
📚 PART 3 · A DYNASTY THAT LOOKED TO THE PAST

Egypt's own story continues past the Kushite pharaohs of Week 10's first lesson. After Assyria's sack of Thebes in 663 BC weakened Kushite rule, a new Egyptian dynasty rose from the Delta city of Sais, one Egyptologists call the Saite period (664 to 525 BC), and Herodotus's own visit to Egypt likely came during or shortly after this era, which helps explain how much he was able to see and record.

The Saite pharaohs are worth noticing for a reason this course has flagged before: not everything in history moves forward. Saite artists and architects deliberately studied and imitated the styles of the Old Kingdom, the pyramid age this course met all the way back in Week 5, copying ancient tomb inscriptions and reviving nearly thousand-year-old artistic conventions on purpose. This was not backwardness or a failure of imagination. It was a deliberate choice, a civilization looking to its own deep past for legitimacy and continuity during an uncertain present, precisely the kind of pattern worth remembering: history is not one long climb toward something better, and sometimes a civilization's most confident move is to look backward rather than forward.
