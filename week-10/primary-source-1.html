The Great Triumphal Stele of Piye
Primary Source 1
The Great Triumphal Stele of Piye
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from Egyptological translations of the granite stele Piye had erected at Napata (Jebel Barkal) around 727 BC, recording his campaign against the northern Egyptian prince Tefnakht and other Delta rulers. The stele is unusually long and detailed for a royal victory inscription, narrating the campaign almost as a continuous story rather than a simple list of conquests.
📖 THE STELE

On Piye's reason for marching north:
"His majesty sent to the princes and army commanders of Egypt: hasten to battle, take your positions, draw near to fight, surround, and capture the people within their own towns. Waste no time. But wash yourselves in the river before the battle, and dress in fine linen. Do not fight without cause. Offer to my father Amun before you advance, that he may deliver our enemies into our hands."

On the fate of a defeated prince's horses:
"When the prince Namlot brought forth his horses to be inspected, his majesty's heart grieved for them, more than for any other matter of that day. His majesty said: it is more painful to my heart that these horses have suffered hunger than any wrong you have done in pursuit of your own ambitions. Let it be known that the stables of my enemies shall be inspected, and their neglect of these creatures counted against them."

On princes who submitted from a distance rather than approaching in person:
"Then came the princes of the north, prostrating themselves in the manner of dogs, saying, 'Be at peace with us.' They did not enter into the presence of his majesty, for they were unclean, eaters of fish, an abomination to the palace. Yet they sent forth their gifts, silver and gold and every good and precious thing, and his majesty accepted these offerings from where they stood."

On the campaign's outcome:
"Then all the land was at peace under the feet of this god. All the princes lay upon their bellies before him, and Lower Egypt and the fortresses of the north opened their gates to him. Every man went about rejoicing, and every heart glad, for the terror of his majesty had entered every land."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
The stele closes with Piye departing for Napata, in the Kushite south, rather than remaining in Egypt to govern its northern territories directly, leaving local princes in place under his overarching authority, a detail this week's Lesson 1 discusses directly.
