💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Herodotus's Histories, Book II, written in the fifth century BC. These excerpts are chosen deliberately to include both Herodotus's careful, plausible eyewitness reporting and his more credulous traveler's tales, so that the two can be compared directly.
📖 THE HISTORIES, BOOK II

On Egypt's priests as a source of information:
"Concerning Egypt I will now speak at length, because it possesses more wonders than any other land, and more monuments that defy description. I questioned the priests of Egypt closely, and where I could, I saw things for myself, and I will set down what I was told, noting plainly where I myself believe it and where I do not."

On the flooding of the Nile (careful, reasoned reporting):
"Concerning the source of the Nile's flooding, no one, Egyptian, Libyan, or Greek, could give me a satisfactory account. Some say melting snow causes it, which I judge impossible, since the river flows from the hottest parts of the world. Having weighed what I have seen and heard, my own opinion is that the flood results from the sun's changing position through the seasons, drawing water differently at different times of year. I offer this only as my own reasoned opinion, not as certain fact."

On the preparation of the dead (careful, plausible reporting):
"Those Egyptians who wish to preserve a body properly first remove certain internal organs through an incision, then fill the body with spices, and cover it with a preserving substance called natron for a fixed number of days, after which it is washed and wrapped in fine linen. There are simpler and cheaper methods as well, for those unable to afford the finest treatment."

On a phoenix said to visit the temple of the sun (an openly doubted tall tale):
"The Egyptians also tell of a sacred bird called the phoenix, which I myself have never seen except in paintings, for it is said to visit their country only once every five hundred years, when its father dies. It is said to carry its father's body, wrapped in myrrh, all the way from Arabia to the temple of the sun, and there to bury it. I repeat this only as the Egyptians themselves tell it. I do not vouch for its truth."

On winged serpents guarding frankincense trees (a claim Herodotus presents as his own firsthand proof):
"I traveled to a certain place in Arabia to learn about winged serpents said to guard the trees that produce frankincense, and there I saw for myself an enormous quantity of their bones and ribs, piled in heaps of every size. Whether this proves the creatures were truly as described to me, I leave the reader to judge."
