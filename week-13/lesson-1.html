📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What is a city built to say, not just built to hold?

Nineveh fell to a combined Babylonian-Median army in 612 BC, an event Week 12 described in detail. Babylon, one of the two victors, was now free to build its own empire in the vacuum Assyria's collapse had left behind. Historians call this the Neo-Babylonian Empire, and its greatest king, Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605 to 562 BC), turned Babylon itself into one of the most famous cities of the ancient world.

📚 PART 1 · A CITY AS POLITICAL STATEMENT

This is a city this course has already visited once before, under a very different king, Hammurabi, all the way back in Week 4. Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon was a far larger and grander place, and he was determined that visitors, subjects, and rivals alike would know it. His building inscriptions, this week's second Primary Source, describe rebuilding the city's massive walls, its royal palace, and above all its ceremonial gateway, the Ishtar Gate, covered in glazed blue bricks and decorated with rows of sculpted bulls and dragons sacred to Babylonian gods. Nearby stood the Etemenanki, a towering ziggurat dedicated to Babylon's chief god, Marduk, a structure so tall and so famous that later Greek visitors to Babylon described it in terms that may have influenced the biblical story of the Tower of Babel.

It is worth being direct about what a project like this is actually for. A ziggurat or a glazed gateway is not primarily a practical structure the way a granary or a fortified wall is. It is a political statement in stone and colored brick, a deliberate display meant to announce that Babylon's king, and Babylon's gods, deserved awe and obedience. This is not a new idea in this course. Egyptian pyramids in Week 5 and Assyrian palace reliefs in Week 11 served a similar purpose in different forms. What makes Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon distinctive is its sheer scale and the deliberate use of color and imported material, cedar from Lebanon, glazed brick technique perfected specifically for these projects, to make the statement as vivid as possible.

📚 PART 2 · AN EMPIRE, NOT JUST A CITY

Nebuchadnezzar's ambitions were not confined to architecture. His armies expanded Neo-Babylonian control across much of the territory Assyria had once ruled, including a thirteen-year siege of the Phoenician city of Tyre, a campaign Week 15 will discuss further, and repeated campaigns into the region historians call the Levant, along the eastern Mediterranean coast. Among the small kingdoms in that region was Judah, ruled from the city of Jerusalem, whose fate under Nebuchadnezzar is the direct subject of this week's second lesson.

⚠️ A WARNING WORTH REMEMBERING
Before moving forward, it is worth recalling a lesson Week 12 just finished teaching. Assyria, too, had once looked unstoppable, its capital seemingly permanent, its power seemingly total, only to collapse within a single generation once its internal unity failed. Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon is genuinely magnificent, and this lesson has described that magnificence honestly. But nothing about that magnificence is a guarantee of permanence. Babylon's own turn to face the same kind of test Assyria failed is not far off, a story this course will return to directly in Week 17. For now, Lesson 2 turns to one specific consequence of Nebuchadnezzar's power: the destruction of Jerusalem and the beginning of a period Jewish tradition still remembers as the Babylonian exile.
