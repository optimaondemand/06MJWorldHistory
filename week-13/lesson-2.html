📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What happens to a people's identity when its kingdom, temple, and homeland are all taken away at once?

This course has met the kingdoms of Israel and Judah only in passing so far, through Shalmaneser III's Black Obelisk in Week 11. This lesson gives them the fuller attention their history deserves, both as a small kingdom caught between great powers and as a genuinely distinct set of religious beliefs worth understanding on their own terms.

📚 PART 1 · A KINGDOM STANDING ALONE

By the seventh century BC, the northern kingdom of Israel no longer existed as an independent state at all. Assyria had destroyed it in 722 BC and deported much of its population, a policy this course has already seen Assyrian kings use to prevent conquered peoples from organizing future rebellions. That left only the smaller southern kingdom, Judah, ruled from Jerusalem, maneuvering as best it could between Egyptian and Mesopotamian power for over a century afterward. Judah's independence finally ended under Nebuchadnezzar II. This week's first Primary Source, drawn from a Babylonian chronicle, records his forces capturing Jerusalem in 597 BC and carrying off its king and much of its elite population to Babylon. When a later Judean uprising tested Nebuchadnezzar's patience further, his response was final: in 586 BC, Jerusalem itself was destroyed, along with its Temple, the central site of Israelite worship, and a further wave of its population was deported to Babylon, beginning the period Jewish tradition remembers as the Babylonian exile.

📚 PART 2 · A BELIEF WORTH UNDERSTANDING ON ITS OWN TERMS

This course has studied several religious systems so far, Sumerian, Egyptian, and Mesopotamian more broadly, each recognizing many gods with different responsibilities, often tied closely to nature, kingship, or specific cities. Israelite religion, as reflected in its own surviving texts, worked differently. It centered on monotheism, devotion to a single god, and on a covenant, a binding, mutual agreement between that god and the Israelite people specifically, in which loyalty and obedience to a shared law were understood to carry consequences, for better or worse, in the community's actual historical fortunes. This is a genuinely distinct religious commitment, not simply the same kind of belief as its neighbors with the names changed, and it deserves to be taken seriously as its own thing rather than flattened into a version of Mesopotamian or Egyptian religion. At the same time, this course does not claim to settle, one way or another, any question of religious truth. What can be said with confidence, as a matter of history rather than theology, is that Israelite writers themselves frequently interpreted their kingdom's defeats, including this one, as directly connected to this covenant relationship, a framework worth understanding honestly on its own terms rather than translating into some other civilization's categories.

🎯 TRY IT: TWO KINDS OF BELIEF
Sort each feature by the religious system it describes.
Many gods, each often tied to a particular city, natural force, or aspect of kingship.

📚 PART 3 · AN ENDING THAT WAS NOT, IN FACT, AN ENDING

Exile is a form of forced migration, and it might reasonably have meant the permanent disappearance of Judah's people and beliefs into the wider Babylonian population, the way conquered peoples sometimes did disappear from the historical record entirely. That is not what happened here. Religious and cultural identity persisted through decades of exile in Babylon itself, kept alive without a king, a temple, or a homeland to anchor it, a genuinely unusual outcome worth noting rather than assuming was inevitable. Its restoration, when it came, arrived from an unexpected direction: in 538 BC, a new conqueror, having just taken Babylon itself, issued a decree permitting Judean exiles to return home and rebuild their Temple, a policy markedly different from anything this course has studied from Assyria or Babylon. That conqueror's name was Cyrus, and his own remarkable rise, and his very different approach to holding an empire together, is where this course turns in full in Week 17.
