💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the Babylonian Chronicle covering Nebuchadnezzar II's seventh regnal year (598 to 597 BC), which records the first Babylonian capture of Jerusalem in its characteristically terse, factual style.
📖 THE CHRONICLE

On the campaign itself:
"In the seventh year, in the month of Kislev, the king of Babylon called out his troops and marched to the land of Hatti [the region including the Levant]. He set up camp against the city of Judah, and on the second day of the month of Adar he captured the city and seized its king."

On the outcome:
"He appointed a king of his own choosing there, and he took heavy tribute and brought it back to Babylon."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE, IMPORTANT FOR ACCURATE HISTORICAL UNDERSTANDING
This surviving chronicle tablet documents the first Babylonian capture of Jerusalem, in 597 BC, when Nebuchadnezzar deported the young king Jehoiachin and much of the city's elite population but left Jerusalem itself standing under a new, Babylonian-appointed king. The Babylonian Chronicle series has a significant gap in its surviving tablets covering the years immediately afterward, so the final, complete destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BC, described in Lesson 2, is known primarily from the Hebrew Bible's own historical books and later classical writers, not from a surviving Babylonian chronicle tablet describing that specific event. Historians are careful to keep these two distinct moments, 597 and 586 BC, and these two different kinds of evidence, separate rather than blending them together.
