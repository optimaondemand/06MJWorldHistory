💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Herodotus's Histories, Book I, describing Cyrus's contested origins and his conquest of Lydia.
📖 THE HISTORIES, BOOK I

On Astyages's dream and its consequences:
"Astyages, king of the Medes, dreamed that his daughter's son would one day rule in his place. In fear, he ordered the infant killed. But the herdsman entrusted with the deed took pity on the child and raised him secretly as his own, and the boy grew to manhood as Cyrus, without Astyages ever learning the truth until his grandson had already grown too powerful to stop."

On Solon's visit to Croesus:
"Croesus, proud of his vast treasury, asked his Athenian guest Solon whom he considered the happiest of all men, expecting to hear his own name. Solon instead named two obscure citizens who had lived honorably and died well, and when Croesus pressed him further, Solon answered: 'Count no man happy until you have seen how his life ends, for fortune is fickle, and often overturns those who seem most secure.'"

On the war with Cyrus:
"Croesus, confident in his wealth and his army, marched against the rising power of Cyrus without heeding Solon's warning. He was defeated, his capital city of Sardis captured, and his kingdom absorbed into the growing Persian empire."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Herodotus wrote this account roughly a century after the events it describes, and the conversation between Solon and Croesus, however memorable, cannot be confirmed as a real historical meeting. Herodotus is best read here, as with the accounts of Deioces in Week 14, not as a certain factual record but as evidence of how later Greeks understood, and drew moral lessons from, the sudden reversal of Lydia's fortune at Cyrus's hands.
