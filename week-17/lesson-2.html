📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What actually happened when Babylon fell, and what does the record it left behind really say?

In 539 BC, Cyrus's army took Babylon, the same magnificent city this course toured through Nebuchadnezzar's Ishtar Gate back in Week 13. According to ancient tradition, the city fell with remarkably little resistance, its own king, Nabonidus, deeply unpopular with Babylon's priesthood for neglecting the worship of the chief god Marduk. Whatever combination of military pressure and internal discontent actually decided the outcome, Mesopotamia, the region this course has followed since Sumer and Uruk in Week 2, now belonged to a Persian king for the first time in its history.

📚 PART 1 · WHAT THE CYLINDER ACTUALLY SAYS

This week's first Primary Source is the Cyrus Cylinder, a clay cylinder inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform and buried in Babylon's foundations, discovered by archaeologists in 1879. In recent decades, this object has earned a striking modern reputation, sometimes described as the world's first human rights charter, and even displayed for a time at the United Nations under that label. This course needs to look at that claim honestly, and skeptically.

Read on its own terms, in the genre it actually belongs to, the Cyrus Cylinder is a fairly conventional Mesopotamian royal inscription, closely related in style and purpose to Nebuchadnezzar's own building inscriptions this course studied in Week 13. It presents Cyrus as chosen by the god Marduk himself to rescue Babylon from Nabonidus's misrule, describes the restoration of neglected temples, and boasts of Cyrus's own piety and generosity, exactly the kind of self-praising claims nearly every Mesopotamian king before him made about his own reign. It is not a constitution. It does not describe universal rights belonging to all people simply for being human, a modern idea that would have made little sense in this world at all.

📚 PART 2 · A REAL AND CONSEQUENTIAL DIFFERENCE

What genuinely deserves this course's attention is not the Cylinder's language, but the real policy behind it. The inscription describes Cyrus returning displaced peoples to their home cities and restoring their captured cult statues to their proper temples, and outside evidence confirms this reflects an actual, deliberate Persian practice, not empty royal boasting. This stands in real contrast to Assyrian deportation, which this course studied scattering the northern Kingdom of Israel permanently in Week 11, and to Babylon's own exile policy, which this course studied removing Judah's elite to Babylon in Week 13. This same policy is exactly what allowed the Judean exiles, already introduced in Week 13, to return home to Jerusalem and begin rebuilding their Temple, a direct and consequential payoff of a decision Cyrus made as part of this same broader approach to ruling a newly won, multi-ethnic empire.

💡 A QUESTION WORTH ARGUING BOTH WAYS
This course has now studied two very different approaches to holding a large, diverse empire together. Assyria, in Weeks 11 and 12, relied on calculated terror, and collapsed with shocking speed the moment its strongest king was gone. Cyrus relies instead on tolerance: restoring local gods, permitting return from exile, and, as later weeks of the Achaemenid story will show further, generally allowing conquered peoples to keep their own customs and local officials under Persian oversight. Does this genuinely bind an empire together better than terror does, solving the first-order political problem of holding a vast, culturally varied territory in lasting peace? Or is it simply too early to tell, a policy that has not yet faced the kind of real test that broke Assyria apart? This course will not settle that question outright. Weigh the evidence from both empires honestly, and be ready to argue it either way.
📖 CLOSING

Cyrus himself would not live to answer this question. That task, and the next great conquest, falls to his successors, beginning with the Persian ruler this course meets directly in Week 18, who turns Persia's attention toward the last river valley left to bring under this new empire's rule: Egypt.
