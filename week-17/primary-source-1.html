💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the Cyrus Cylinder, a clay cylinder inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform and buried in Babylon's foundations following Cyrus's conquest of the city in 539 BC.
📖 THE CYRUS CYLINDER

On Cyrus's chosen status:
"Marduk, the great lord, surveyed all the lands in search of a righteous ruler, and called Cyrus, king of Anshan, by name, appointing him to rule over all the earth. Marduk looked with favor on Cyrus's good deeds and his righteous heart, and commanded him to march against Babylon."

On the entry into Babylon:
"Without any battle, Marduk caused Cyrus to enter Babylon, sparing the city hardship. Nabonidus, the king who had not revered him, Marduk delivered into Cyrus's hands."

On restoring displaced peoples:
"I gathered together the former inhabitants of the cities across the region of Sumer and Akkad, whom Nabonidus had brought to Babylon against the will of the gods, and I returned them, unharmed, to their own dwellings. I likewise returned the images of their gods, which had been brought to Babylon, to their proper shrines."

On restoring worship:
"I sought to strengthen the sanctuaries of all the gods, and I gathered together their scattered inhabitants and returned them to their homes."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
The Cyrus Cylinder has become widely, and inaccurately, described in modern times as the world's first human rights charter, and was even displayed at the United Nations under that description. Read in its own historical context, it is a fairly conventional Mesopotamian royal building and restoration inscription, closely related to Nebuchadnezzar's inscriptions studied in Week 13, presenting Cyrus's conquest and policies as pleasing to Marduk in exactly the self-praising style nearly every Mesopotamian king used about his own reign. What is genuinely significant is not its language but its substance: outside evidence confirms that Cyrus really did pursue a deliberate policy of returning displaced peoples and restoring their gods and shrines, a real and consequential difference from Assyrian and Babylonian practice studied earlier in this course.
