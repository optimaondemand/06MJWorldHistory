📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does the start of Cyrus's story sound like something this course has already heard twice before?

This course left the Medes at the height of their power back in Week 14, ruling a kingdom strong enough to help destroy Assyria and negotiate as an equal with wealthy Lydia. That same lesson closed with a warning: the Median royal house was not destined to hold power much longer, and would be overthrown not by a foreign enemy, but by its own grandson. That grandson's name was Cyrus, and his rise begins, once again, with a story this course has now heard several times before.

📚 PART 1 · ANOTHER INFANT, ANOTHER IMPROBABLE SURVIVAL

According to Herodotus, King Astyages of Media, Cyaxares's own descendant, dreamed that his daughter's newborn son would someday overthrow him. Terrified, Astyages ordered the infant killed. The herdsman given this grim task instead spared the child, raising him secretly as his own, and the boy grew up as Cyrus, a Persian vassal youth, without Astyages ever learning the truth until it was already too late to matter. However this story actually began, it should sound immediately familiar. This course has now met an abandoned or endangered infant, secretly saved and raised in obscurity before rising far beyond anyone's expectation, in Sargon's basket back in Week 3, in Romulus and Remus's river rescue in Week 15, and now in Cyrus. This course should say directly what it has only implied before: this is a recognizable kind of story, appealing across cultures with no contact at all, and its repeated appearance should make any student rightly cautious about taking any single version as reliable, word-for-word history.

🎯 TRY IT: WHO'S WHO IN THE DREAM
Match each description to the person it names in Herodotus's account.
The Median king whose dream convinced him his own infant grandson would one day overthrow him.
The infant secretly spared by a herdsman and raised in obscurity, who grew up to lead a revolt against his own grandfather.

Whatever the truth of his childhood, the adult Cyrus led a Persian revolt that overthrew Astyages around 550 BC, uniting Media and Persia under his own rule for the first time. This union marks the true beginning of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, named for Cyrus's royal line, an empire that will, within this course's remaining weeks, grow larger than anything the ancient Near East had yet seen.

📚 PART 2 · CROESUS, SOLON, AND A WARNING IGNORED

Cyrus's next target lay to the west: Lydia, a wealthy Anatolian kingdom this course already met briefly in Week 14, ruled now by King Croesus, whose name became a byword for riches in the ancient world. Herodotus preserves a memorable story about Croesus, set some years before his war with Cyrus. According to this account, the Athenian lawgiver Solon, already introduced to this course through his reforms in Week 16, visited Croesus's court, where the king proudly showed off his treasury and asked Solon to name the happiest man alive, plainly expecting Solon to name him. Solon instead named two obscure private citizens who had each lived well and died honorably, and when Croesus pressed him, Solon offered a warning rather than flattery: count no man happy until his life has actually ended, since fortune can turn on anyone, however wealthy, at any time.

Croesus, Herodotus tells us, dismissed the warning as foolishness. Confident in his own wealth and power, he went to war against the rising Persian empire and lost decisively, his kingdom conquered and, in most versions of the story, his own life placed in genuine danger before Cyrus spared him. Whether or not this specific conversation with Solon ever really happened, and Herodotus himself was writing generations after the fact, it captures a piece of Greek moral thinking this course should take seriously on its own terms: worldly success is not the same thing as a settled, secure happiness, and pride in present fortune has a long history of coming before a fall.

📖 CLOSING

Lydia's conquest in 547 BC brought the wealthy Ionian Greek coastal cities, the same cities Homer's descendants and neighbors called home, under Persian rule for the first time, a detail this course flagged as coming in Week 16. Cyrus's empire, barely a few years old, had already reached from the Zagros Mountains to the shores of the Aegean Sea. Lesson 2 turns to its next, and most consequential, conquest.
