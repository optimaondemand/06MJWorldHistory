The Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty (1259 BC)
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from translations of the treaty concluded between Ramesses II of Egypt and Hattusili III of the Hittites, preserved both in an Egyptian hieroglyphic version carved at Karnak and an Akkadian cuneiform version found at Hattusa. It is the oldest surviving peace treaty in world history whose full text has come down to us.
📖 THE TREATY

On the treaty's purpose:
"The great chief of Hatti and the great ruler of Egypt have made this agreement, so that good peace and good brotherhood shall exist between them forever, so that the sons of the sons of the great chief of Hatti shall remain at peace and in brotherhood with the sons of the sons of the great ruler of Egypt, unto eternity."

A mutual promise never to invade:
"As for the future, from this day forward, the great chief of Hatti shall never again enter the land of Egypt, to take anything from it. And Ramesses, the great ruler of Egypt, shall never again enter the land of Hatti, to take anything from it."

A pledge of mutual defense:
"If any other enemy comes against the lands of the great ruler of Egypt, and he sends word to the great chief of Hatti saying, 'Come with me as reinforcement against him,' the great chief of Hatti shall come, and the great chief of Hatti shall fight against that enemy. And if it does not please the great chief of Hatti to come himself, he shall send his infantry and his chariots to fight the enemy. Likewise, if any enemy comes against the land of Hatti, the great ruler of Egypt shall come to fight on the great chief of Hatti's behalf, in the same manner."

On internal rebellion, treated as a shared concern:
"If men flee from the land of Egypt to the land of Hatti, or subjects of the great chief of Hatti flee to the land of Egypt, whether they be one man or three men, they shall be returned to whichever ruler they belong, and no case shall be brought against them for their own crimes, nor shall their houses, wives, or children be destroyed on account of their flight."

A closing invocation of divine witnesses:
"A thousand gods and goddesses of the land of Hatti, together with a thousand gods and goddesses of the land of Egypt, are witnesses to these words. As for the one who shall not keep these words, may the gods of Hatti and the gods of Egypt destroy his house, his land, and his servants. As for the one who shall keep these words, may the gods of Hatti and the gods of Egypt grant him life, health, and prosperity, together with his house, his land, and his servants."
