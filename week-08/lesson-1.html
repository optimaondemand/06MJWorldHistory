📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What do two empires do after neither one can win?

This week turns to a region this course has not yet visited directly: Anatolia, the peninsula that makes up most of modern Turkey, and the Aegean world of Crete and mainland Greece just across the sea from it. Three civilizations rose here during the same centuries Egypt's New Kingdom pharaohs were campaigning abroad: the Hittites in Anatolia, and the Minoans and Mycenaeans further west. This lesson introduces the Hittites; Wednesday's lesson turns to the Minoans and Mycenaeans.

📚 PART 1 · ANATOLIAN BEGINNINGS

Anatolia had been farmed since deep antiquity. Einkorn wheat, the world's very first domesticated cereal, was grown in this same region as far back as 9000 BC, part of the Fertile Crescent story Week 1 introduced, though the Hittite state itself would not exist for thousands of years afterward. Around 1650 BC, a Hittite kingdom centered on the massively fortified city of Hattusa began to consolidate power across central Anatolia. Hittite scribes adopted cuneiform, borrowing it from Akkadian scribal tradition much as Weeks 2 through 4 traced cuneiform's spread across Mesopotamia, and used it to write the Hittite language, the oldest attested member of the Indo-European language family, the same broad language family that eventually includes Greek, Latin, and English.

📚 PART 2 · AN EMPIRE BUILT BY CONQUEST

Hittite power reached its height under kings like Suppiluliuma I, who around 1350 BC extended Hittite authority deep into Syria, defeating the powerful kingdom of Mitanni and bringing the Hittites into direct, sometimes hostile contact with Egypt's own sphere of influence in the Levant. Hittite kings ruled as both political and religious heads of their state, and negotiated on the world stage as self-declared equals to Egypt's pharaoh, using the diplomatic title "Great King," a title only a small handful of the era's most powerful rulers could plausibly claim for themselves.

📚 PART 3 · KADESH: THE LARGEST CHARIOT BATTLE IN HISTORY

That rivalry between Hatti and Egypt came to a head in 1274 BC at the Battle of Kadesh, fought in modern Syria between Ramesses II of Egypt and Muwatalli II of the Hittites. Both sides deployed thousands of horse-drawn chariots, likely making Kadesh the largest chariot battle ever fought in world history. Both empires' own accounts claim victory, a useful reminder that royal inscriptions are rarely neutral witnesses to their own outcomes. What actually followed the battle, however, was neither a clear Egyptian triumph nor a clear Hittite one: years of continued tension, followed eventually by something neither side had achieved through years of fighting.

💡 A DIFFERENT KIND OF ENDING
In 1259 BC, Ramesses II and the new Hittite king Hattusili III concluded a formal peace agreement, the Egyptian-Hittite Peace Treaty, the oldest surviving peace treaty in world history, this week's Primary Source. Rather than one side simply overpowering the other, both empires, worn down by years of costly conflict neither could decisively win, chose a negotiated settlement establishing mutual non-aggression and, notably, a promise of mutual defense against outside threats and the return of political refugees. This is worth holding up as a genuine case study in how lasting peace actually gets made: two exhausted great powers, having exhausted the military option, chose the harder, less glorious work of negotiated peace over the continued costs of open war, judging that peace better served the practical interests of both states than any further campaigning could.
📚 PART 4 · IRON, RECONSIDERED

One more Hittite achievement deserves a closer look: early ironworking. For decades, popular accounts described the Hittites as guarding a strict monopoly on iron-smelting technology as a closely held state secret. More recent scholarship has revised this picture considerably. Iron-working knowledge during the Hittite period appears to have developed more gradually and to have been more widespread across the region than the old "secret monopoly" story suggested, and iron would not actually replace bronze as the dominant metal everywhere until after the disruptions of the Bronze Age Collapse, still two lessons away in this course, cut off the long-distance tin trade that bronze production depended on. The Hittites deserve credit as early, skilled ironworkers, but not for a tightly guarded monopoly that the evidence no longer supports.
