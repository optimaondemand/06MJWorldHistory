💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English prose translations of Homer's Iliad, Book 2, the section commonly called the Catalogue of Ships. The full catalogue names nearly thirty contingents gathered to sail against Troy; the selection below preserves several of its most significant entries, including its two largest and most detailed.
📖 THE CATALOGUE

Introducing the catalogue:
"Tell me now, you Muses who dwell on Olympus, for you are goddesses, present at all things and knowing all things, while we mortals know nothing but what news reaches us and know not even that clearly; tell me, then, who were the chiefs and leaders of the Greeks. As for the common soldiers, so many were they that I could not name them nor count them, not even if I had ten tongues and a voice that never grew tired, unless the Muses of Olympus, daughters of Zeus, were to recall to me all those who came beneath the walls of Troy."

Mycenae, led by Agamemnon:
"Those who held Mycenae, the well-built city, rich in gold, and Corinth, and Cleonae, and Sicyon, where Adrastus reigned before Agamemnon ruled there, and those who held the seaboard cities and wide Helike; these were led by Agamemnon, son of Atreus, with a hundred ships, the most of any contingent gathered here, and himself the mightiest of all the chiefs, for he brought by far the greatest army of any."

Sparta, led by Menelaus:
"Those who held Lacedaemon, lying low among its hollow hills, and Sparta, and Messe, home of doves, were led by Menelaus, brother to Agamemnon, with sixty ships. He mustered his men apart from his brother's, himself burning to avenge the theft of Helen, and to bring about the toil and struggle it had cost them all."

Pylos, led by Nestor:
"Those from Pylos, the sandy city, and from Amphigeneia, and Pteleos, and Helos, were led by the old horseman Nestor, wisest counselor among the Greeks, with ninety ships. His years had not dimmed the honor his fellow chiefs paid to his judgment, for age had made him no less valuable in council than in war."

Ithaca, led by Odysseus:
"Those who held Ithaca, and Neritum with its trembling forest leaves, and Crocylea, and rugged Aegilips, were led by Odysseus, the equal of Zeus in counsel, with twelve red-painted ships."

The Myrmidons, led by Achilles:
"Those who held Pelasgian Argos, and Alos, and Alope, and Trachis, and Phthia, and Hellas, home of lovely women, were called Myrmidons, Hellenes, and Achaeans; their fifty ships were led by Achilles. But these men had, for now, no interest in the din of war, for there was no one to lead them into battle: Achilles, swiftest of runners, lay idle beside his ships, angry over the girl Briseis, whom he had taken as the fruit of hard fighting."

Closing the roll call:
"These, then, were the chiefs and rulers of the Greeks. Tell me now, Muse, who among them was noblest, and whose horses were the best, of all those who followed the sons of Atreus."
