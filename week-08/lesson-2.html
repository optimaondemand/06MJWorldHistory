📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why would a Bronze Age capital build no walls at all?

While the Hittites built their empire across Anatolia, two related but distinct civilizations were developing in the Aegean world: the Minoans on the island of Crete, and the Mycenaeans on the Greek mainland. Together they represent the Bronze Age foundation of a region that will matter enormously to world history, though its own classical age still lies well beyond this course's Fall semester.

📚 PART 1 · KNOSSOS AND AN UNWALLED PUZZLE

Minoan civilization, named by modern archaeologists for the legendary King Minos, developed on Crete from around 3000 BC, reaching its height in a palace-centered society by roughly 1700 BC. The largest Minoan palace, at Knossos, was an enormous, sprawling complex containing storage magazines, workshops, and living quarters, equipped with genuinely advanced plumbing: terracotta pipes, flushing latrines, and light-wells bringing sunlight deep into the structure, among the most sophisticated domestic water management found anywhere in the Bronze Age world.

Here is Knossos's real puzzle, though, one worth sitting with rather than explaining away: unlike nearly every other major Bronze Age capital this course has studied, Knossos was built with no defensive walls at all. Historians still debate why. Perhaps Crete's naval strength made land walls unnecessary, protected instead by ships patrolling the surrounding sea. Perhaps Minoan society was organized differently than its heavily fortified neighbors, with less to fear from internal rivals. The honest answer, again, is that no single explanation has won out, and this remains an open question much like the missing palaces of the Indus Valley from last week.

💡 A SCRIPT PARTLY UNDERSTOOD
Minoan palace administrators kept records in a script called Linear A, still undeciphered today, though its numerals, a decimal counting system, can be read even though the underlying language cannot. This is a useful reminder that a writing system can be partially understood, its numbers legible while its words remain locked away, a distinction worth remembering the next time an undeciphered script comes up in this course.
📚 PART 2 · MYCENAE AND THE WANAX

On the Greek mainland, a related but separate civilization, the Mycenaeans, rose to prominence from around 1650 BC, eventually absorbing Crete into their own sphere of influence by around 1450 BC. Mycenaean palace centers, at sites including Mycenae, Tiryns, and Pylos, were organized around a ruler called the wanax, at the head of a detailed bureaucratic hierarchy of officials and scribes. Those scribes wrote in Linear B, adapted from Linear A to record an early form of the Greek language itself, the earliest attested Greek writing in existence, though it would remain undeciphered until 1952, when the architect and self-taught linguist Michael Ventris finally cracked it. Overwhelmingly, what Linear B tablets actually record are numbers: sheep, chariots, grain, land, all counted and tracked for palace bookkeeping. This is the palace economy in its clearest form, a centrally planned and redistributive system in which the palace itself collected, stored, and reallocated much of a region's agricultural and craft production, workers and goods flowing in and out of the palace's own accounts rather than trading freely on an open market.

Unlike unwalled Knossos, Mycenae itself was heavily fortified, protected by massive Cyclopean walls, built from stones so large that later Greeks, unable to imagine ordinary humans lifting them, credited their construction to the mythical one-eyed Cyclopes. Mycenaean elite burial included impressive tholos tombs, beehive-shaped stone chambers built to house the bodies and grave goods of Mycenae's ruling families, a monumental architecture of death to match the grandeur of the palace itself in life.

📚 PART 3 · A WAR AT THE EDGE OF MEMORY

Greek tradition remembered a great war fought by a coalition of Mycenaean kings against the city of Troy, traditionally dated to around 1184 BC by the ancient scholar Eratosthenes. This week's Primary Source, an excerpt from Homer's Iliad, lists the ships and contingents that tradition says sailed to fight there. Like Gilgamesh's Uruk or Yu the Great's China, the Trojan War likely reflects some real kernel of Late Bronze Age memory, but the specific cast of characters, the exact date, and the true scale of any such conflict remain genuinely debated rather than confirmed history. Keep this distinction in mind as you read this week's excerpt: a poem composed centuries after the events it describes, drawing on a real palace-economy world this lesson has just introduced, but not a simple historical record of it.
