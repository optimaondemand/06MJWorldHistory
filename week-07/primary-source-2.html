Shang Oracle Bone Inscriptions
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from translations of Shang dynasty oracle bone inscriptions, dating to around the reign of King Wu Ding, roughly 1250 BC. Each inscription follows a similar pattern: a date, the name of the diviner who prepared the bone, a question posed to the royal ancestors or spirits, and sometimes a recorded outcome added afterward.
📖 THE INSCRIPTIONS

On the harvest:
"Crack-making on this day: the diviner Que divined: 'This year, the harvest of millet will be a good one.' The king read the cracks and said: 'It will be good. There will be a great harvest.'"

On the king's health:
"Crack-making: the diviner divined: 'The king's toothache is not caused by the ancestor Father Yi.' Further divined: 'It is caused by Father Geng instead. An offering should be made to him.'"

On weather and travel:
"Crack-making: divined: 'This coming ten-day week there will be no disaster.' The king read the cracks and said: 'There may be trouble. It may come from outside.' On the sixth day, word arrived from the west that raiders had struck a border settlement, just as the king had judged."

On Lady Hao's childbirth:
"Crack-making: the diviner Que divined: 'Lady Hao's coming childbirth will be good.' On the day the child was born, it was recorded: 'It was not good. It was a daughter.'"

On Fu Hao's military command:
"Crack-making: divined: 'The king should order Lady Fu Hao to lead the troops in an attack against the Tu Fang.' Divined: 'In this campaign, Lady Fu Hao will receive divine assistance.'"

💡 A NOTE ON THIS LAST SET OF INSCRIPTIONS
Oracle bone questions about childbirth being described as "good" only when a son was born, as in the entry above, reflect the value Shang court culture placed on male heirs, a detail worth noticing rather than passing over, even as the same royal family relied on a woman, Fu Hao, to command its armies in the field.
