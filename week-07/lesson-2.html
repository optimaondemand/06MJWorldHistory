📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What can a cracked bone tell a historian three thousand years later?

Far to the east of the Indus Valley, along the Yellow River in northern China, a very different kind of river valley civilization was taking shape by around 1600 BC: the Shang dynasty, the first Chinese dynasty confirmed by both archaeology and its own writing. Chinese tradition remembers an earlier dynasty, the Xia, founded by the legendary flood-taming hero Yu the Great, but no confirmed written or archaeological evidence for the Xia has yet been found, so historians treat it, like Gilgamesh's Uruk or the Trojan War, as legendary or semi-legendary rather than confirmed history. The Shang dynasty is a different matter entirely: its kings, its capital cities, and even individual named people are documented in the civilization's own surviving written records.

📚 PART 1 · TALKING TO THE ANCESTORS

Those records take an unusual form: oracle bones, the flat shoulder bones of cattle or the undersides of turtle shells, prepared by Shang diviners working for the royal court. A diviner would apply a heated point to the bone or shell, causing it to crack, and would then interpret the pattern of the crack as an answer from the royal ancestors or gods to a question the king wished to ask, questions about the harvest, about whether to go to war, about a coming illness, or about the meaning of a dream. Afterward, scribes carved the original question, and sometimes the outcome, directly onto the same bone in written characters.

This practice, called divination, gives modern historians something extraordinarily rare: a huge archive, tens of thousands of inscribed bones have survived, of a Bronze Age royal court's own day-to-day concerns, written in its own hand. Oracle bone script, dated to around 1250 BC under a king named Wu Ding, is the first confirmed, undisputed Chinese writing system, and it is the direct ancestor of the Chinese characters still used today, thousands of years later.

📚 PART 2 · ANYANG AND A WARRIOR QUEEN

Around this same time, the Shang capital settled at Anyang, known archaeologically as Yinxu, where extensive royal tombs have been excavated. One of the most remarkable belonged to Fu Hao, a consort of King Wu Ding, whose tomb, discovered still sealed and unlooted in 1976, revealed an enormous store of ritual bronze vessels, jade objects, and weapons. Oracle bone inscriptions referring to Fu Hao describe her personally leading military campaigns on the king's behalf, commanding thousands of troops, an unusually direct and well-documented example of a woman wielding real military authority in the ancient world, worth comparing to Hatshepsut's very different path to power in Egypt two weeks ago.

📚 PART 3 · BRONZE AT AN EXTRAORDINARY SCALE

Shang China also produced ritual bronze vessels of remarkable technical sophistication, cast using a distinctive method called piece-mold casting, different from the lost-wax casting techniques used in the Near East, and capable of producing some of the largest and most intricately decorated bronze objects made anywhere in the ancient world. These vessels were not everyday tools but ritual objects, used to make offerings of food and wine to royal ancestors, whose favor and guidance, as the oracle bones show, the Shang kings sought constantly.

⚠️ A CIVILIZATION UNTOUCHED BY DISTANT COLLAPSE
One more point is worth holding onto as this course's Quarter 1 draws toward its close. Next week's lessons turn to the palace civilizations of the eastern Mediterranean, and Week 9 will describe a wave of crisis and collapse that devastates much of the Near East around 1200 to 1150 BC. Shang China, thousands of miles away with no direct contact with that world, is entirely unaffected by it, and its dynasty continues on for another century beyond that collapse. Keep this in mind as a useful check against assuming any single historical event, however dramatic, was a truly global one.
