📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What happens when a civilization leaves behind cities, but no king?

This week steps outside the Near East for the only time in the Fall semester, to look briefly at two civilizations developing farther afield: the Indus Valley, in what is now Pakistan and northwest India, and Shang China, along the Yellow River far to the east. Neither will receive the full, ongoing treatment this course has given Sumer and Egypt. Both civilizations belong properly to units of their own, which you will study in more depth in a future semester. But both deserve a real look now, as genuine river valley civilizations in their own right, and as a chance to compare how differently civilization can take shape when it develops with little or no contact with Mesopotamia and Egypt.

📚 PART 1 · A CIVILIZATION BUILT ON A RIVER, LIKE THE OTHERS

Farming reached the region around the Indus River independently, with wheat, barley, and a distinctive local species of cattle, the zebu, domesticated by around 7000 BC at sites like Mehrgarh in the hill country bordering the river plain. Over the following millennia, this early farming culture grew into a full urban civilization, reaching its height in what historians call the Mature Harappan period, roughly 2600 to 1900 BC, named for Harappa, one of its two greatest cities, the other being Mohenjo-daro. In 1924, the British archaeologist Sir John Marshall announced the discovery of this entire lost civilization to the world for the first time. This week's Primary Source draws on Marshall's own excavation report, describing what his team found at Mohenjo-daro.

📚 PART 2 · REMARKABLY PLANNED CITIES

What Marshall's excavations revealed was startling: cities laid out on a precise grid of straight streets, built from bricks manufactured to standardized sizes across the entire civilization's territory, an area larger than either Mesopotamia or Egypt at the time. Mohenjo-daro's houses connected to covered brick drains running beneath the streets, and many had private wells and what appear to be early bathing rooms, a level of urban planning and public sanitation that would not be matched again in much of the world for centuries. At the port city of Lothal, archaeologists even identified what may be one of the earliest tidal dockyards built anywhere, evidence of long-distance maritime trade that reached as far as Mesopotamia itself, where Sumerian texts refer to a trading partner called "Meluhha," widely believed to be the Indus civilization.

💡 A GENUINE, UNRESOLVED PUZZLE
Here is where the Indus Valley becomes genuinely strange, compared to every other civilization this course has studied so far. Despite years of careful excavation, no royal palace, no grand temple, and no monumental royal tomb has ever been identified anywhere in Indus territory. No inscription naming a king, a dynasty, or even a single individual ruler has ever been found. The Indus script itself, which appears on small carved seals and pottery, remains undeciphered to this day, and scholars still debate whether it even records a full spoken language or something closer to a system of symbols and marks. Very little evidence of warfare, fortification built for defense against other Indus cities, or weapons stockpiled in quantity has turned up either.
📚 PART 3 · WHY THE PUZZLE MATTERS

This lesson will not pretend to resolve what professional archaeologists themselves have not resolved. Some historians suggest Indus cities were ruled by some kind of collective body, a council of merchants or priests, rather than a single king. Others suspect evidence of kingship simply has not survived, or has not yet been found. Still others wonder whether Indus society was organized on genuinely different principles than the kingdoms and empires this course has focused on, something without a clean parallel among its Bronze Age contemporaries. The honest answer, at this point in the story, is that nobody knows for certain, and a good historian says so plainly rather than inventing a confident answer the evidence does not support.

This is worth taking seriously as its own kind of lesson. It would be easy to assume every ancient civilization must have looked something like Sumer or Egypt: a king, a temple, a monumental tomb. The Indus Valley shows that civilization, meaning cities, planned infrastructure, specialized labor, and long-distance trade, does not automatically require the same political shape everywhere it appears. Keep this open question in mind as Wednesday's lesson turns to Shang China, a river valley civilization that, unlike the Indus, left abundant written evidence naming its kings by name.
