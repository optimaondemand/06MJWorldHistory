📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What does it look like to keep ordinary records right up to the end of your civilization?

Ramesses III's Medinet Habu inscription, this week's first Primary Source, tells the Bronze Age Collapse's story from the winning side, a great power that survived a direct assault and lived to commemorate its own victory. This lesson's Primary Source tells the same crisis from the losing side, through the ordinary paperwork of a palace administration that had no idea it was living through its final weeks.

📚 PART 1 · THE VIEW FROM PYLOS

At Pylos, one of the Mycenaean palace centers introduced last week, archaeologists recovered a set of clay tablets, hardened and preserved, ironically, by the very fire that destroyed the palace around 1180 BC. Among them are records historians call the rower tablets: administrative lists organizing men, likely rowers for warships, and assigning officers to watch posts along the coastline. These are not poetry, not royal boasts, not even a clear statement of what danger the palace expected. They are exactly the kind of dry, practical bureaucratic record a palace economy, as Week 8 described, produced constantly as a matter of routine: names, numbers, place assignments. Read alongside what we now know happened next, they take on an unmistakable weight. This was a government mobilizing its coastal defenses, most likely in genuine anticipation of a seaborne threat, in the final months before its palace burned and Mycenaean civilization in this region ended for good.

📚 PART 2 · A GOVERNMENT THAT DID NOT KNOW ITS OWN ENDING

This is worth sitting with directly. Whoever wrote these tablets was doing ordinary administrative work, the same kind of work Linear B scribes had been doing for generations, counting men and assigning posts, with no sense that they were recording the last acts of a civilization. History rarely announces its own turning points in advance. The people living through the Bronze Age Collapse experienced it, in the moment, as a series of ordinary decisions, urgent threats, and administrative responses, not as "the Bronze Age Collapse," a label historians would only apply looking backward, centuries later, once the full pattern of loss across an entire region became clear.

📚 PART 3 · WHAT ENDURES, AND WHAT DOES NOT

As Quarter 1 closes, it is worth taking stock of what this crisis actually swept away, and what it did not. The Hittite Empire ceases to exist as a political power; Hattusa itself is destroyed and never rebuilt as an imperial capital. Mycenaean palace civilization, with its wanax-centered bureaucracy and Linear B literacy, collapses so completely that writing itself disappears from Greece for centuries, a dark period this course will meet again in Week 16. Kassite Babylon falls as well, opening a Mesopotamian power vacuum that Weeks 10 through 17 will trace in detail, as new powers rise to fill it. Egypt and the rest of Mesopotamia survive, wounded, their reach and wealth diminished, but their civilizations continue. The Levant, meanwhile, is resettled in these same turbulent decades by new arrivals, including the Philistines, likely connected to the Sea Peoples themselves, and by the tribal groups that Week 13 will describe becoming the kingdom of Israel. And along the coast, one people is positioned, almost by accident of survival and geography, to inherit much of the long-distance Mediterranean trade network Mycenaean sailors once ran: Phoenicia, whose own remarkable story begins in earnest in Week 15.

Nothing about any of this was planned, or owed to any civilization by the shape of history itself. Some things ended. Some things endured, changed by what they survived. And some new things were only now becoming possible, in a Near East that would look, within a century, nothing like the world these first nine weeks have described.
