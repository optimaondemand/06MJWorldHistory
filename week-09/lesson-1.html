📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What happens when several great powers collapse at once, and nobody agrees on why?

For eight weeks, this course has followed civilizations rising, expanding, and competing across the ancient Near East and beyond: Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, Egypt, the Indus Valley, Shang China, the Hittites, and the palace worlds of Crete and Mycenae. This week, nearly all of it comes apart at once. Between roughly 1200 and 1150 BC, in the span of just a few decades, the eastern Mediterranean world experienced one of the most dramatic collapses in recorded history, and historians still do not agree on exactly why.

📚 PART 1 · WHO WERE THE SEA PEOPLES?

Egyptian records from this period describe repeated attacks by a shifting coalition of seaborne raiders and migrants that modern historians call the Sea Peoples, a label invented by nineteenth-century scholars rather than a name these groups used for themselves. Egyptian inscriptions name several distinct peoples within this coalition, but their exact origins, whether from the Aegean, Anatolia, the central Mediterranean, or several places at once, remain genuinely debated among historians today. What is not in doubt is the timing: these attacks intensify sharply in the very years that the Hittite Empire, Mycenaean Greece, and Kassite Babylon all collapse or suffer catastrophic damage.

Around 1208 BC, shortly before this wider crisis, the Egyptian pharaoh Merneptah had already fought off a coalition including Libyan raiders and groups later associated with the Sea Peoples, commemorating his victory on a stele that happens to contain the earliest known reference anywhere to a people called "Israel" living in Canaan, a detail this course will return to directly in Week 13. But the more serious crisis was still to come. This week's Primary Source, the Medinet Habu inscription of Ramesses III, describes a much larger Sea Peoples assault reaching Egypt's own borders roughly a generation later, one Egypt survived, but at lasting cost.

⚠️ A CASE STUDY IN HISTORIOGRAPHY
This is a moment where being honest about the evidence matters more than telling a tidy story. It would be tempting to tell this story as a single clean narrative: one cause, one villain, one clear turning point. The evidence will not support that kind of story. Historians have proposed drought and prolonged famine, weakening royal economies and provoking migration and raiding. Others point to internal political failure, rebellions, and breakdowns in the elaborate trade networks that bronze production itself depended on, since bronze requires tin, and tin had to travel enormous distances to reach Bronze Age workshops. Still others describe a kind of systems collapse, in which several of these pressures combined and reinforced each other, so that no single cause alone would have been enough to bring down so much at once, but several together proved unstoppable. The honest, current scholarly answer is that probably several of these factors were operating together, in ways that are still not fully understood and may never be resolved with total confidence.
📚 PART 2 · EGYPT, WOUNDED BUT ALIVE

This is one of the clearest examples anywhere in the ancient world of how messy real historical change can be. There is no march toward inevitable progress here, no plan unfolding as it should. Great powers, the Hittite Empire above all, simply stop existing within a matter of decades. Others, Egypt and Mesopotamia among them, survive, deeply wounded, never quite the same civilizations they were before. History does not owe anyone a tidy explanation, and a good historian resists inventing one where the evidence genuinely will not support it.

Ramesses III's forces did succeed in repelling the Sea Peoples assault, in a major land and naval battle the Medinet Habu inscription describes and commemorates in vivid detail. But victory came at a real cost. Egypt's New Kingdom survived the crisis, but its finances, territory, and international influence never fully recovered, and the following century sees Egyptian power steadily receding, a story Week 10 will pick up directly. Wednesday's lesson turns to a very different kind of source from this same crisis: not a triumphant royal inscription, but a set of ordinary administrative records from a palace that did not survive.
