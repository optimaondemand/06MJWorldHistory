The Legendary Founding of Rome
Primary Source 2
The Legendary Founding of Rome (Romulus and Remus, per Livy)
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Livy's Ab Urbe Condita ("From the Founding of the City"), Book 1, describing the legendary founding of Rome.
📖 AB URBE CONDITA, BOOK 1

On the twins' danger and exposure:
"Amulius, having seized the throne of Alba Longa from his brother Numitor, feared vengeance from Numitor's line, and above all from his daughter Rhea Silvia's newborn twin sons, Romulus and Remus. He ordered the infants placed in a basket and cast into the flooded Tiber River, trusting the waters to finish what he dared not do outright."

On their rescue:
"The basket, rather than sinking, came to rest on dry ground as the floodwaters receded, near a fig tree at the foot of the Palatine Hill. There, a she-wolf, coming down to drink, found the infants and suckled them until a shepherd named Faustulus discovered the pair and carried them home to raise as his own."

On the decision to found a new city:
"Grown to manhood and having restored their grandfather Numitor to his rightful throne, Romulus and Remus resolved to found a city of their own near the place where they had first washed ashore. But the brothers disagreed sharply over exactly where the new city's walls and sacred boundary should be marked, each favoring a different hill."

On the founding and the killing that followed:
"As Romulus marked out the line of the new wall, Remus leaped over it in mockery, scorning so low a barrier. Enraged, Romulus struck him down, declaring, 'So shall it be henceforth for anyone else who dares to leap over my walls.' Thus Rome was founded, and thus, from its very first day, it was stained with a brother's blood."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Livy wrote this account roughly seven centuries after the traditional founding date of 753 BC, drawing on generations of Roman legend rather than any contemporary record. Modern historians generally treat Romulus and Remus themselves as legendary rather than historical figures, though archaeological evidence does confirm that a genuine settlement existed on the Palatine Hill by roughly this period. Notice how closely the twins' river rescue echoes Sargon's basket story from Week 3, a pattern worth naming rather than treating as coincidence.
