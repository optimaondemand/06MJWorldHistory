📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What do two rival cities look like before either one knows the other exists?

Somewhere in the eighth century BC, by the traditional reckoning of the ancient world itself, two cities were founded on opposite shores of the central Mediterranean. Neither city was much to look at yet. Neither founder had any idea the other city existed, let alone what it would someday become. Centuries later, their descendants would fight three long wars to decide which of them would survive at all. This week's two Primary Sources tell each city's own legendary account of how it began.

📚 PART 1 · DIDO AND THE NEW CITY

This week's first Primary Source concerns Carthage, the greatest of the Phoenician colonies Lesson 1 introduced, planted on the North African coast not far from modern Tunis. Its own name, in Phoenician, meant simply Qart-hadašt, "New City." According to the oldest surviving version of its founding story, preserved by the later writer Justin, a Tyrian princess named Dido, sometimes called Elissa, fled her home city after her brother, King Pygmalion of Tyre, murdered her husband for his wealth. Dido sailed west with a band of loyal followers and, through a mix of shrewd bargaining and outright trickery with the local Berber ruler over how much land her new settlement could claim, established Carthage on the North African shore. Ancient tradition dates this founding to 814 BC.

Centuries later, the Roman poet Virgil told a very different version of Dido's story in his epic the Aeneid, written under the emperor Augustus. In Virgil's telling, Dido falls in love with the Trojan hero Aeneas, blown ashore at Carthage while fleeing the fall of Troy, and kills herself in despair when he sails on toward Italy to found the line that will one day become Rome. This version is a magnificent piece of literature, but it cannot be literal history, and this course should say so plainly. The Trojan War, whatever its historical kernel, belongs to an era centuries before 814 BC, the very Bronze Age this course studied collapsing back in Week 9. Virgil knew this. He was not making a mistake. He was writing several centuries after Rome and Carthage had already fought their real wars, and he used this deliberate anachronism, a detail placed in the wrong period on purpose, to suggest that Rome's later hatred of Carthage was somehow written into the two cities' fates from the very beginning. It is a poet's argument, not a historian's claim, and the difference matters.

📚 PART 2 · ROMULUS, REMUS, AND A CITY BUILT ON A KILLING

This week's second Primary Source turns to Rome's own founding legend, preserved most fully by the Roman historian Livy. According to Livy, twin brothers named Romulus and Remus were born to Rhea Silvia, whose uncle Amulius had seized the throne of the nearby city of Alba Longa from her father, Numitor, and forced her into a role that was meant to leave her childless. Fearing the twins as future rivals, Amulius ordered them drowned in the Tiber River. The basket carrying them instead washed ashore near the Palatine Hill, where, tradition says, a she-wolf nursed them until a shepherd named Faustulus found and raised them as his own.

Notice how closely this echoes a story this course already knows well. An infant set adrift on a river, saved against every likelihood, and destined for far more than his humble rescuers could have guessed, is almost exactly the shape of Sargon's basket story from Week 3. This is not evidence that one story copied the other. It is evidence that this particular kind of story, a low, uncertain beginning concealing a great destiny, appealed to ancient audiences again and again, in cultures with no contact at all.

Match each name to who they are in Livy's account.

The rightful ruler of Alba Longa, overthrown by his own brother, later restored to his throne by his grandsons.

Grown to manhood, Romulus and Remus restored their grandfather Numitor to his throne, then set out to found a city of their own near where they had washed ashore. Livy's account does not end happily. The brothers quarreled bitterly over exactly where the new city's sacred boundary line, the pomerium, should run. When Remus mockingly leaped over the low wall Romulus had begun to mark out, Romulus, in Livy's telling, killed his own twin brother on the spot, declaring that the same fate awaited anyone else who dared cross Rome's boundary uninvited. Tradition dates this founding, and this fratricide, to 753 BC. Rome, in other words, begins its own story with an act of violence its own historians never tried to hide.

📖 CLOSING

Set side by side, these two founding stories share a striking pattern: exile, hardship, and a small, uncertain settlement built by people fleeing worse circumstances elsewhere. Neither Dido's followers nor Romulus's shepherds could have imagined that their two cities would someday stand as the Mediterranean's two greatest rival powers, locked in a struggle that would not begin in earnest for another five and a half centuries. That struggle lies well beyond this course's Fall Semester. What belongs here is the beginning: two foundling cities, born the same rough century, each certain only of its own small patch of shore.
