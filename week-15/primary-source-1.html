The Legendary Founding of Carthage
Primary Source 1
The Legendary Founding of Carthage (Dido, per Virgil and Justin)
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations and summaries of Justin's Epitome of the Philippic History of Pompeius Trogus and Virgil's Aeneid, Books 1 and 4, describing two different ancient traditions about Carthage's founding.
📖 TWO TRADITIONS ABOUT DIDO

Justin's account, on why Dido fled Tyre:
"Pygmalion, king of Tyre, murdered Acherbas, the husband of his own sister Elissa, coveting his great wealth. Elissa, learning of the crime, gathered a band of nobles who shared her fear and hatred of the tyrant, loaded ships with gold, and secretly fled the city by night."

Justin's account, on founding the new city:
"Arriving on the coast of Africa, Elissa's company won the friendship of the local inhabitants through trade, and asked to purchase only so much land as could be covered by an ox hide. Cutting the hide into the thinnest possible strips, she claimed a hill overlooking the harbor, upon which the citadel of the new city was built. The city that grew up around it was called Carthage, meaning 'New City' in the Phoenician tongue."

Virgil's account, on Dido and Aeneas, written centuries later:
"When the Trojan hero Aeneas, fleeing the destruction of his own city, was cast ashore near Carthage, Queen Dido received him with honor and, before long, with love. Yet the gods had decreed that Aeneas must sail on to Italy to found the people from whom Rome would one day rise. When he departed, Dido, in her grief and fury, mounted a great pyre and took her own life, calling down a curse of eternal enmity between her people and his descendants."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
These two traditions come from very different times and purposes. Justin's version, tracing back to the earlier Greek historian Timaeus, preserves what is likely the older, more historically grounded core of Carthage's founding story, dated by ancient tradition to 814 BC. Virgil wrote his version roughly eight hundred years later, under the Roman emperor Augustus, and deliberately placed Dido in the same era as the Trojan War, a period centuries before Carthage actually existed. This is not a historical error on Virgil's part. It is a poet's device, using an impossible meeting between Dido and Aeneas to suggest that Rome and Carthage's later rivalry, which Roman audiences already knew the outcome of, was somehow fated from the very beginning.
