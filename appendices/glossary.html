💡 HOW TO USE THIS GLOSSARY
Every term below was introduced in bold somewhere in the Fall Semester's 18 weeks. Each entry names the week (or weeks) where the term first appears in the lessons, so you can go back and reread that section if you want more context.
A
Achaemenid (Week 17)
the name, taken from Cyrus the Great's royal line, given to the Persian Empire he founded by uniting Media and Persia.
Achilles (Week 16)
the greatest warrior fighting at Troy in the Iliad, whose wounded pride leads him to withdraw from battle at great cost to his own side, even though his courage as a fighter is never in doubt.
Aeneas (Week 15)
the Trojan hero in Virgil's later literary version who falls in love with Dido at Carthage before sailing on to found the line that would become Rome.
Agamemnon (Week 16)
the overall commander of the Greek expedition at Troy, whose seizure of Achilles's captive Briseis sets off the quarrel that opens the Iliad.
Akkad (Week 3)
the capital city founded by Sargon for his empire, whose exact location has never been rediscovered by archaeologists even though it is remembered in written records.
Akkadian (Week 3)
a Semitic language, distinct from Sumerian, that Sargon and his successors promoted alongside Sumerian in official business across their empire.
alphabet (Week 15)
the Phoenician writing system of about twenty-two simple symbols, each standing for a single consonant sound, far easier to learn than hieroglyphics or cuneiform; Greek communities later adopted and adapted it (Week 16).
amelu (Week 4)
under Hammurabi's Code, free persons of high or full legal status, who received different penalties and protections than other classes for the same offense.
Amorite (Week 4)
a semi-nomadic, Semitic-speaking people who migrated into Mesopotamia from the western desert and the Levant beginning around 2000 to 1900 BC, gradually settling among and eventually ruling existing Mesopotamian cities, including Babylon.
Amulius (Week 15)
the uncle who seized the throne of Alba Longa from his brother Numitor and ordered the infant Romulus and Remus drowned out of fear they would grow up to challenge him.
anachronism (Week 15)
a detail deliberately placed in the wrong time period, as when Virgil had the Trojan Aeneas meet Dido even though the Trojan War occurred centuries before Carthage's founding.
annals (Week 6)
an official, year-by-year style of historical record, like the Annals of Thutmose III inscribed on the temple walls at Karnak, describing a ruler's military campaigns in unusually specific detail for an ancient source.
Anyang (Week 7)
the site, known archaeologically as Yinxu, where the Shang capital settled and where extensive royal tombs, including Fu Hao's, have been excavated.
archaeology (Week 1)
the careful study of the physical remains people leave behind, such as buildings, tools, seeds, bones, and trash, used to learn about periods with no written records.
Archaic Greece (Week 16)
the era following the Greek Dark Age, beginning around 800 BC, when Greece's population recovered, Mediterranean trade picked back up, and Greeks adopted and adapted the Phoenician alphabet.
Aruna Pass (Week 6)
the narrow, single-file mountain pass toward Megiddo that Thutmose III chose over his officers' objections, gambling that the enemy would not expect an attack from that direction.
Ashurbanipal (Week 12)
the Assyrian king (ruled roughly 668 to 627 BC) under whom the empire reached its widest extent, who was personally taught to read and write cuneiform, and who gathered a great royal library at Nineveh.
Ashurnasirpal II (Week 11)
the Assyrian king (ruled roughly 883 to 859 BC) who rebuilt Assyria's army, moved the capital to Nimrud, and used publicized, calculated terror as a deliberate instrument of imperial policy.
Assyria (Week 11)
a long-established Mesopotamian regional power, dating back well before Hammurabi's Babylon, that Ashurnasirpal II rebuilt into the dominant Neo-Assyrian Empire.
Astyages (Week 17)
the king of Media, grandson of Cyaxares, who ordered the infant Cyrus killed after dreaming his grandson would overthrow him, and who was later overthrown by Cyrus around 550 BC.
Avaris (Week 6)
the capital city in Lower Egypt from which the Hyksos ruled.
B
Babylonian Chronicle (Week 12)
a Babylonian record, written in a terse, factual style with little editorializing, that documents the campaign and siege that brought about the fall of Nineveh in 612 BC.
Babylonian exile (Week 13)
the period beginning when Nebuchadnezzar II destroyed Jerusalem and its Temple in 586 BC and deported much of Judah's population to Babylon.
bard (Week 16)
a professional oral storyteller who recited and refined tales like those behind the Iliad and Odyssey aloud, generation after generation, before they were ever written down.
Battle of Kadesh (Week 8)
a 1274 BC battle fought between Ramesses II of Egypt and Muwatalli II of the Hittites, likely the largest chariot battle in world history, with both sides' own records claiming victory.
Behistun Inscription (Week 18)
an inscription carved high into a cliff face at Mount Behistun, commissioned by Darius I, that records his version of how he took the Persian throne and later became the key to deciphering cuneiform.
Black Obelisk (Week 11)
a tall, four-sided stone monument set up by Shalmaneser III at Nimrud recording tribute received from five foreign rulers, including Jehu of Israel.
Bronze Age Collapse (Week 9)
the dramatic collapse or severe damage suffered by many eastern Mediterranean civilizations, including the Hittite Empire and Mycenaean Greece, within a few decades around 1200 to 1150 BC, a crisis historians still do not fully agree on the causes of.
bronze (Week 2)
an alloy of copper and tin that held a sharper edge than copper alone and helped push Mesopotamia into the Bronze Age by around 3000 BC.
Byblos (Week 15)
one of Phoenicia's three most important city-states.
C
Cambyses II (Week 18)
the son of Cyrus the Great who inherited the Persian Empire in 530 BC and conquered Egypt in 525 BC, defeating Psamtik III at Pelusium.
Carthage (Week 15)
the greatest of the Phoenician colonies, founded on the North African coast and, by tradition, established in 814 BC by the exiled Tyrian princess Dido.
Catalogue of Ships (Week 8)
the section of Homer's Iliad, Book 2, listing the ships and contingents that tradition says sailed from Greece to fight at Troy.
century (Week 1)
a period of one hundred years, part of this course's vocabulary for discussing dates that stretch far into the past.
city-state (Weeks 2, 15)
a single city and its surrounding territory governed as one independent unit, Sumer's answer to the problem of organizing enough people to build and maintain a shared irrigation system; Phoenicia, later in the course, was likewise organized as a set of independent, self-governing coastal cities rather than one kingdom.
civilization (Week 2)
a society marked by dense population, monumental architecture, specialized labor, social stratification, and writing all appearing together, as they did for the first time at Uruk.
code of law (Week 4)
a comprehensive written collection of laws, like Hammurabi's, covering areas of daily life such as property, family, and labor, inscribed publicly so subjects could know the standards they could appeal to.
colony (Week 15)
a settlement Phoenician traders planted along their trade routes, such as in North Africa, Sicily, Sardinia, and near the Strait of Gibraltar, to secure raw materials like tin.
colophon (Week 12)
a short scribal note at the end of a tablet identifying its owner and sometimes warning future readers against removing or damaging it.
copper (Week 2)
a metal worked in Mesopotamia since the Ubaid period just before Uruk's rise, used alongside older stone tools before bronze became widespread.
covenant (Week 13)
a binding, mutual agreement between the Israelite god and the Israelite people, in which loyalty and obedience to a shared law were understood to carry real consequences for the community's fortunes.
Croesus (Week 17)
the king of Lydia, famous for his wealth, who ignored Solon's warning about the uncertainty of fortune and lost his kingdom to Cyrus's Persian army.
Curse of Akkad (Week 3)
a Sumerian literary composition mourning the fall of the Akkadian Empire, which explains the collapse as divine punishment after Naram-Sin destroyed Enlil's temple, bringing famine and the invading Gutians.
cuneiform (Week 2)
the wedge-shaped writing system developed in Sumer, made by pressing a reed stylus into wet clay, which grew out of a system of counting tokens used to track temple goods.
Cyaxares (Week 14)
the Median king (ruled 625 to 585 BC) under whom the Medes joined Babylon in destroying Nineveh in 612 BC and later fought a long war with Lydia.
Cyclopean walls (Week 8)
the massive fortification walls of Mycenae, built from stones so large that later Greeks credited their construction to the mythical one-eyed Cyclopes.
Cyclops (Week 16)
a one-eyed giant, like Polyphemus, encountered by Odysseus and his crew in the Odyssey.
Cyrus Cylinder (Week 17)
a clay cylinder inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform, buried in Babylon's foundations and discovered in 1879, that describes Cyrus restoring displaced peoples and their temples after conquering Babylon.
Cyrus the Great (Week 17)
the Persian king who, according to Herodotus, survived an infant assassination order, overthrew his grandfather Astyages of Media around 550 BC, and went on to conquer Lydia and Babylon, founding the Achaemenid Empire.
Çatalhöyük (Week 1)
a Neolithic town in what is now central Turkey, excavated by James Mellaart starting in the 1960s, notable for having no streets, wall-to-wall mudbrick houses entered through roof holes, and wall installations of cattle-skull bucrania.
D
Darius I (Week 18)
the Persian king who emerged from a succession crisis after Cambyses II's death in 522 BC, killed a man he claimed was an impostor named Gaumata, and left behind his own account of these events carved into the Behistun Inscription.
decade (Week 1)
a period of ten years, part of this course's vocabulary for discussing dates that stretch far into the past.
decipherment (Week 18)
the process of working out how to read an unknown or forgotten writing system, made possible for Babylonian cuneiform by comparing it to the already-understood Old Persian portion of the Behistun Inscription.
Deioces (Week 14)
according to Herodotus, a Median villager trusted for his fairness in settling disputes who was chosen as the Medes' first king, though historians remain uncertain how much of his story is real history.
depopulation (Week 16)
the sharp, lasting drop in Greece's population during the Greek Dark Age, one of the clearest signs that this period was a genuine decline rather than a pause.
deshret (Week 5)
the ancient Egyptians' name for the "red land," the desert beyond the fertile Nile floodplain.
Dido (Week 15)
a Tyrian princess, also called Elissa, who, by tradition, fled Tyre after her brother murdered her husband and went on to found Carthage.
divination (Week 7)
the Shang practice of interpreting the pattern of a crack in a heated bone or shell as an answer from the ancestors or gods to a question the king wished to ask.
domestication (Week 1)
the process by which people selectively bred wild plants and animals for traits they wanted, such as larger seeds or tamer behavior, turning wild species into farmed crops and livestock.
dynasty (Week 3)
a line of rulers connected by family, such as the Akkadian rulers who followed Sargon, including his grandson Naram-Sin.
E
Ecbatana (Week 14)
the capital Deioces is said to have built, with concentric walls of different colors and strict royal ceremony meant to keep the nobles from feeling too familiar with him.
eclipse (Week 14)
a solar event that turned day briefly to night during a battle between the Medes and Lydians, stopping the fighting because both armies took it as an omen they did not want to defy.
Elamite (Week 18)
one of the three languages and cuneiform scripts used in the Behistun Inscription alongside Old Persian and Babylonian.
empire (Week 3)
a single political power ruling over many different, previously separate peoples, cities, and languages at once, as Sargon's Akkadian Empire did across Mesopotamia, distinct from a kingdom that rules one people in one place.
en / ensi (Week 2)
the titles used for the temple administrators of a Sumerian city like Uruk, who tracked grain, wool, and livestock coming into the temple and directed how it was given back out.
epic (Week 2)
a long poem built up over centuries celebrating the journeys and deeds of a hero, such as the Epic of Gilgamesh, which historians do not treat as literal fact even when its hero was also a real king.
Etemenanki (Week 13)
a towering ziggurat in Babylon dedicated to the chief god Marduk, so famous that later Greek descriptions of it may have influenced the biblical story of the Tower of Babel.
excavation (Week 1)
the slow, layer-by-layer removal of soil at an archaeological site, with archaeologists recording exactly where every object was found and at what depth, since depth usually shows relative age.
F
Fertile Crescent (Week 1)
a curved band of hill country running from the eastern Mediterranean coast through Turkey and down into Mesopotamia, where wild ancestors of wheat, barley, lentils, chickpeas, sheep, goats, cattle, and pigs all overlapped, making it the birthplace of farming.
fratricide (Week 15)
the killing of one's own sibling, as when Romulus killed his twin brother Remus in the legendary founding of Rome, traditionally dated to 753 BC.
Fu Hao (Week 7)
a consort of the Shang king Wu Ding whose sealed, unlooted tomb revealed a rich store of bronze vessels, jade, and weapons, and whose oracle bone inscriptions describe her personally leading military campaigns.
G
Great King (Week 8)
a diplomatic title Hittite kings used to declare themselves equals to Egypt's pharaoh, one only a small handful of the era's most powerful rulers could plausibly claim.
Greek Dark Age (Week 16)
the roughly three-and-a-half-century period, about 1150 to 800 BC, after the Bronze Age Collapse, when Greece's population fell sharply, its palace centers were abandoned, long-distance trade dried up, and writing itself was lost.
H
Halys River (Week 14)
the river in Anatolia that became the agreed border between the Medes and Lydia after their war ended.
Harappa (Week 7)
one of the two greatest cities of the Indus Valley civilization, which gives its name to the Mature Harappan period.
Hatshepsut (Week 6)
a New Kingdom pharaoh who came to power around 1479 BC, first as regent and then as pharaoh in her own right for roughly two decades, best remembered for a major trading expedition to Punt rather than military conquest.
Hattusa (Week 8)
the massively fortified city that became the center of Hittite power in central Anatolia starting around 1650 BC.
Henry Rawlinson (Week 18)
the British officer and scholar who, in the 1830s and 1840s, risked his life scaling the cliff at Mount Behistun to copy the inscription by hand, work that helped scholars learn to read Babylonian cuneiform.
Herodotus (Week 10)
a Greek writer from Halicarnassus, often called the "Father of History," who traveled to Egypt roughly two centuries after Piye and wrote about it in his Histories, questioning his sources and noting his own uncertainty rather than simply repeating everything as settled fact.
historiography (Week 3)
the study of why civilizations decline and who gets to say why, examined this week by comparing the Curse of Akkad's own moral explanation for Akkad's fall against modern historians' material explanations like drought and invasion.
Homer (Week 16)
the poet traditionally credited with composing the Iliad and the Odyssey, though scholars are divided over whether one person wrote them or whether the name represents a long tradition of many bards.
hubris (Weeks 3, 16)
a person's or a king's overreaching pride, blamed by the Curse of Akkad for inviting divine punishment on Naram-Sin, and shown again later when Odysseus cannot resist shouting his true name back to the blinded Polyphemus, undoing some of his own cleverness through vanity.
Hyksos (Week 6)
a Semitic-speaking people who took control of Lower Egypt from their capital at Avaris and ruled as a foreign, partly Egyptianized dynasty from about 1650 to 1550 BC, remembered for introducing the horse-drawn chariot and the composite bow.
I
Iliad (Week 16)
the epic poem attributed to Homer that opens on the quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon and examines the costs of Achilles's wounded pride.
Indus script (Week 7)
the still-undeciphered writing system of the Indus Valley civilization, found on small carved seals and pottery, with scholars still debating whether it records a full spoken language or something closer to symbols.
Instructions of Ptahhotep (Week 5)
one of the oldest surviving wisdom texts in the ancient world, framed as advice from an aging Egyptian vizier to his son and successor about everyday virtues like listening well, controlling one's temper, and treating others without arrogance.
ironworking (Week 8)
early Hittite iron-smelting, once popularly described as a tightly guarded state secret, but now understood by scholars to have developed more gradually and spread more widely than that older story suggested.
Ishtar Gate (Week 13)
Babylon's grand ceremonial gateway, rebuilt by Nebuchadnezzar II and covered in glazed blue bricks decorated with sculpted bulls and dragons sacred to Babylonian gods.
J
Jehu (Week 11)
a king of Israel shown on the Black Obelisk prostrating himself before Shalmaneser III and presenting tribute, one of the very few surviving ancient images of a named Israelite king.
Jericho (Week 1)
an ancient walled settlement near the Jordan River in the Levant, excavated by archaeologist Kathleen Kenyon, where a massive stone tower and wall dating to roughly 8000 BC made it one of the oldest walled settlements ever found, built even before people had invented pottery.
Judah (Week 13)
the small kingdom ruled from Jerusalem that survived after Assyria destroyed the northern kingdom of Israel, until Judah itself was conquered by Nebuchadnezzar II.
K
Kemet (Week 5)
the ancient Egyptians' own name for their land, meaning "the black land," referring to the dark, fertile silt the Nile flood left behind each year.
Knossos (Week 8)
the largest Minoan palace on Crete, an enormous complex with advanced plumbing, notably built with no defensive walls at all, a puzzle historians still debate.
Kush (Week 10)
a kingdom that grew for centuries along the upper Nile south of Egypt, absorbing Egyptian religious practice and hieroglyphic writing so thoroughly that its kings came to see themselves as guardians of Egyptian tradition.
L
lex talionis (Week 4)
Latin for "law of retaliation," the principle at the heart of Hammurabi's Code that punishment should match the harm done, capping what a wronged party could take in revenge rather than leaving retaliation open-ended.
Linear A (Week 8)
the still-undeciphered script used by Minoan palace administrators, whose number system can be read even though its underlying language cannot.
Linear B (Week 8)
the script Mycenaean scribes adapted from Linear A to record an early form of the Greek language, the earliest attested Greek writing, not deciphered until 1952.
Lothal (Week 7)
an Indus Valley port city where archaeologists found what may be one of the earliest tidal dockyards, evidence of long-distance maritime trade reaching as far as Mesopotamia.
Lower Egypt (Week 5)
the northern region of Egypt before unification, centered on the wide, marshy Nile Delta where the river fans out toward the Mediterranean.
Lydia (Weeks 14, 17)
a wealthy kingdom in Anatolia; under King Alyattes it fought a long war with the Medes that ended, after the Battle of the Eclipse in 585 BC, with a new peace and a border at the Halys River, and under his son Croesus it was later conquered by Cyrus the Great in 547 BC.
M
ma'at (Week 5)
the cosmic order, truth, and balance that kept the universe functioning correctly, which the pharaoh was responsible for maintaining.
Marduk (Week 17)
the chief god of Babylon, whose neglect by King Nabonidus and whose supposed favor toward Cyrus are both described in the Cyrus Cylinder.
mastaba (Week 5)
a type of Old Kingdom tomb chamber, decorated with painted and carved scenes of daily life and inscriptions naming the tomb's owner and their titles.
Mature Harappan (Week 7)
the name historians give to the height of Indus Valley civilization, roughly 2600 to 1900 BC, named for the city of Harappa.
Medes (Week 14)
an Iranian-speaking people who settled around the Zagros Mountains after the Bronze Age Collapse and appear only faintly in written records for centuries before helping destroy the Neo-Assyrian Empire.
Medinet Habu (Week 9)
the site of Ramesses III's inscription describing Egypt's costly victory over a much larger Sea Peoples assault a generation after Merneptah's.
Megiddo (Week 6)
a fortified city in modern northern Israel where a coalition of rebellious Canaanite and Syrian rulers gathered against Egypt, and the site of Thutmose III's famous first military campaign.
Mehrgarh (Week 7)
an early farming site in the hill country near the Indus River where wheat, barley, and the zebu were domesticated by around 7000 BC.
Memphis (Week 5)
the new capital founded near the boundary of Upper and Lower Egypt after unification, symbolizing the joining of the two lands.
Merneptah stele (Week 9)
a monument commemorating the Egyptian pharaoh Merneptah's roughly 1208 BC victory over a coalition of raiders, notable for containing the earliest known reference anywhere to a people called "Israel" living in Canaan.
Middle Kingdom (Week 6)
the period of Egyptian history from roughly 2055 to 1650 BC, when Egypt reunified after the First Intermediate Period into an era of renewed stability, expanded trade, and notable literature.
millennium (Week 1)
a period of one thousand years, part of this course's vocabulary for discussing dates that stretch far into the past.
Minoan (Week 8)
the Bronze Age civilization that developed on the island of Crete from around 3000 BC, named by modern archaeologists after the legendary King Minos.
Mohenjo-daro (Week 7)
one of the two greatest cities of the Indus Valley civilization, known for its precise street grid, covered brick drains, private wells, and early bathing rooms.
monotheism (Week 13)
devotion to a single god, the belief at the center of Israelite religion, unlike the many-god systems of Sumer, Egypt, and Mesopotamia studied earlier in the course.
mushkenum (Week 4)
under Hammurabi's Code, a class of free but dependent commoners who had fewer legal protections and different, often lower, payments owed to or by them compared to the amelu.
Mycenaean (Week 8)
the related but separate Bronze Age civilization that rose on the Greek mainland from around 1650 BC and eventually absorbed Crete into its sphere of influence.
N
Nabonidus (Week 17)
the last king of Babylon before Cyrus's conquest in 539 BC, unpopular with Babylon's priesthood for neglecting the worship of Marduk.
Napata (Week 10)
the city around which the kingdom of Kush grew.
Narmer (Week 5)
the ruler most historians identify as having united Upper and Lower Egypt under one crown around 3100 BC.
Narmer Palette (Week 5)
a ceremonial slate carving found at Hierakonpolis that commemorates Egypt's unification, showing a king wearing both the White Crown of Upper Egypt and the Red Crown of Lower Egypt while smiting a bound enemy.
Nebuchadnezzar II (Week 13)
the greatest king of the Neo-Babylonian Empire, ruled 605 to 562 BC, who rebuilt Babylon into a dazzling city and conquered Judah.
Neo-Assyrian Empire (Week 11)
the empire Ashurnasirpal II and his successors built up beginning in the ninth century BC, eventually becoming the largest empire the Near East had yet seen.
Neo-Babylonian Empire (Week 13)
the empire Babylon built after helping destroy Assyria in 612 BC, reaching its height under King Nebuchadnezzar II.
Neolithic Revolution (Week 1)
the long, gradual shift from gathering wild food and hunting to deliberately growing crops and herding domesticated animals, beginning in the Fertile Crescent around 9500 BC and unfolding over many generations rather than happening quickly.
New Kingdom (Week 6)
the era of Egyptian history from roughly 1550 to 1150 BC, following the expulsion of the Hyksos, marked by empire, ambitious pharaohs, and rulers like Hatshepsut and Thutmose III.
Nimrud (Week 11)
the rebuilt city that Ashurnasirpal II made his capital.
Nineveh (Week 12)
Ashurbanipal's capital city and the site of his great library, and, as Assyria's final capital, the city destroyed by fire in 612 BC and never rebuilt as an imperial capital.
Numitor (Week 15)
the rightful king of Alba Longa and grandfather of Romulus and Remus, whose throne was seized by his brother Amulius.
O
o-ka tablets (Week 9)
Linear B watch-post records from Pylos assigning officers and companies of men to guard specific points along the coastline.
Odysseus (Week 16)
the hero of the Odyssey, whose cleverness saves his crew from the Cyclops Polyphemus, though his own pride nearly undoes that success at the last moment.
Odyssey (Week 16)
the epic poem attributed to Homer that follows Odysseus's journey home, including his escape from the Cyclops Polyphemus.
Old Kingdom (Week 5)
the period of Egyptian history from roughly 2686 to 2181 BC, marked by strong centralized pharaonic rule and the most ambitious building projects the ancient world had yet attempted, including the Great Pyramid.
Old Persian (Week 18)
one of the three languages and cuneiform scripts used in the Behistun Inscription, and the one scholars understood first, giving them a starting point for deciphering the other two.
oracle bone (Week 7)
a cattle shoulder bone or turtle shell prepared by Shang diviners, cracked with a heated point and then inscribed with a question and sometimes its outcome, used to seek answers from royal ancestors or gods.
oral tradition (Week 16)
stories, genealogies, and songs passed by memory from one generation of storytellers to the next without being written down, the way memory of the Mycenaean world survived through the Greek Dark Age.
P
palace economy (Week 8)
a centrally planned, redistributive economic system, seen clearly in Mycenaean records, in which the palace collected, stored, and reallocated much of a region's agricultural and craft production rather than letting goods trade freely on an open market.
Palatine Hill (Week 15)
the hill near the Tiber River where the basket carrying Romulus and Remus washed ashore and where Rome was later founded.
Pelusium (Week 18)
the border fortress city on the eastern edge of the Nile Delta where Cambyses II's Persian army decisively defeated the Egyptian forces in 525 BC.
pharaoh (Week 5)
the title for Egypt's king, who from very early in Egyptian history was treated as a living god, an embodiment of the falcon god Horus responsible for maintaining ma'at.
Philistines (Week 9)
new arrivals who resettled the Levant in the turbulent decades after the Bronze Age Collapse, likely connected to the Sea Peoples.
Phoenicia (Week 15)
a region of independent trading city-states along the coast of the Levant, rather than a single kingdom, whose sailors became the finest navigators and shipbuilders in the Mediterranean.
piece-mold casting (Week 7)
the distinctive bronze-casting method used in Shang China, different from the lost-wax casting used in the Near East, capable of producing very large and intricately decorated ritual bronze vessels.
Piye (Week 10)
the Kushite king who marched north into Egypt around 727 BC, presenting his campaign as a religious duty to restore ma'at rather than simple conquest, and who returned home to Napata after securing submission rather than staying to govern Egypt directly.
Plimpton 322 (Week 4)
a Babylonian clay tablet listing sets of numbers matching what we now call Pythagorean triples, produced over a thousand years before Pythagoras was born in Greece.
Polyphemus (Week 16)
the one-eyed Cyclops who traps Odysseus and his crew in his cave and eats several of them before being blinded and outwitted.
pomerium (Week 15)
the sacred boundary line Romulus marked out for the new city of Rome, which Remus mockingly leaped over, leading Romulus to kill him.
Psamtik III (Week 18)
the last king of Egypt's Saite Dynasty, defeated by Cambyses II's Persian army at Pelusium in 525 BC.
Punt (Week 6)
a land likely located along the Red Sea coast of modern Eritrea, Sudan, or Somalia, source of the incense, ebony, ivory, gold, myrrh, and other goods Hatshepsut's famous trading expedition brought back to Egypt.
Pylos (Week 9)
a Mycenaean palace center where clay tablets, hardened and preserved by the fire that destroyed the palace around 1180 BC, recorded a government's final administrative acts before its collapse.
R
Remus (Week 15)
according to Livy, the twin brother of Romulus, killed by Romulus after mockingly leaping over the boundary line Romulus was marking out for the new city.
restoration inscription (Week 17)
the kind of conventional Mesopotamian royal inscription the Cyrus Cylinder actually is, describing the restoration of neglected temples and displaced peoples rather than functioning as a modern human-rights document.
Romulus (Week 15)
according to Livy, one of the twin sons of Rhea Silvia, rescued after being set adrift on the Tiber River, who went on to found Rome and kill his own twin brother Remus in a dispute over the city's boundary.
rower tablets (Week 9)
Linear B administrative lists from Pylos organizing men, likely rowers for warships, in what appears to have been a mobilization of coastal defenses shortly before the palace's destruction.
S
Saite Dynasty (Week 10)
the Egyptian dynasty, 664 to 525 BC, that rose from the Delta city of Sais after Assyria's sack of Thebes, known for deliberately reviving Old Kingdom artistic styles and traditions.
satrapy (Week 18)
a province governed under Persian oversight, the status Egypt was reduced to after its conquest by Cambyses II.
scribe (Week 2)
a person trained to read and write cuneiform, considered one of the most valuable and respected professions in Mesopotamian society.
Sea Peoples (Week 9)
a modern label, invented by nineteenth-century scholars rather than a name used by the groups themselves, for a shifting coalition of seaborne raiders and migrants whose attacks coincided with the Bronze Age Collapse; their exact origins remain genuinely debated.
sedentism (Week 1)
the practice of staying in one place year-round instead of moving with the seasons, made possible by farming and treated as the foundation underneath villages, cities, kings, writing, and armies.
serekh (Week 5)
a rectangular frame representing a palace facade, carved on monuments like the Narmer Palette, used to enclose and present a king's name.
sexagesimal (Week 4)
a base-60 number system used in Babylonian mathematics, the same system that still gives us 60 minutes in an hour and 360 degrees in a circle.
Shalmaneser III (Week 11)
Ashurnasirpal II's son, ruled 859 to 824 BC, who extended Assyrian power further and set up the Black Obelisk at Nimrud.
Shang dynasty (Week 7)
the first Chinese dynasty confirmed by both archaeology and its own writing, which developed along the Yellow River beginning around 1600 BC.
Sidon (Week 15)
one of Phoenicia's three most important city-states.
siege engine (Week 11)
military equipment, including siege towers and battering rams, that Assyria's dedicated engineering units used to break through even well-defended city walls.
Sin-shar-ishkun (Week 12)
the Assyrian king recorded as dying during the fall of Nineveh in 612 BC, though the chronicle's terse style leaves some of the exact details uncertain.
social classes (Week 4)
distinct legal and social ranks within a society, such as the amelu, mushkenum, and slaves in Babylon, whose members received different treatment under the same law for the same injury.
Solon (Week 17)
the Athenian lawgiver who, according to Herodotus's story, warned Croesus that no one should be counted happy until their life has actually ended, since fortune can turn on anyone at any time.
standing army (Week 11)
a year-round, professional military force, increasingly equipped with iron weapons and supported by siege engineers and disciplined cavalry, that Assyria built in place of the seasonal levies earlier civilizations like Sumer, Akkad, and Egypt had relied on.
stele (Week 3)
a carved stone monument, such as the one commemorating an Akkadian king's military triumph, used to publicly display royal achievements or, later, laws.
Suppiluliuma I (Week 8)
a Hittite king who, around 1350 BC, extended Hittite authority deep into Syria by defeating the kingdom of Mitanni.
Sumer (Week 2)
the region of southern Mesopotamia, along the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, where the world's first true cities, including Uruk, arose.
surplus (Week 1)
more food than a family needs to survive right now, which farming, unlike foraging, could reliably produce and which allowed some people to stop farming and take on other jobs like potter, builder, priest, or soldier.
systems collapse (Week 9)
the idea that several pressures, such as drought, internal political failure, and broken trade networks, combined and reinforced one another so that no single cause alone explains the Bronze Age Collapse, but several together proved unstoppable.
T
tell (Week 1)
a mound formed when people build, abandon, and rebuild settlements on the same spot for thousands of years, stacking layers of ancient towns on top of one another.
Third Intermediate Period (Week 10)
the era of Egyptian history, roughly 1077 to 664 BC, when the once-unified Egypt this course had followed since Narmer split apart into rival dynasties and power centers.
tholos tomb (Week 8)
a beehive-shaped stone burial chamber used by Mycenaean elites to house the bodies and grave goods of ruling families.
Thutmose III (Week 6)
Hatshepsut's stepson, who became sole ruler of Egypt after her death around 1458 BC and led as many as seventeen military campaigns into the Levant, earning the later nickname "the Napoleon of Egypt."
tolerance (Week 17)
as a matter of imperial policy, Cyrus's approach of restoring local gods, permitting exiled peoples to return home, and letting conquered peoples keep their own customs, offered by this course as a contrast to Assyria's approach of calculated terror.
tribute (Week 11)
regular payments a lesser or defeated power sends to a stronger one to avoid conquest or continued attack.
trilingual (Week 18)
written in three different languages, as the Behistun Inscription was, carved in Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian side by side.
Twenty-fifth Dynasty (Week 10)
the name later historians gave to Piye's line of Kushite pharaohs, who ruled parts of Egypt as far north as the Nile Delta for decades.
Tyre (Week 15)
one of Phoenicia's three most important city-states, later famous for withstanding a thirteen-year siege by Nebuchadnezzar II without being destroyed.
Tyrian purple (Week 15)
an extremely expensive dye extracted drop by drop from the crushed glands of a small sea snail, prized as a mark of royalty for centuries.
U
Upper Egypt (Week 5)
the southern region of Egypt before unification, a string of towns strung along the narrow Nile valley.
V
vassal (Weeks 11, 13, 15, 17)
a ruler or people who submit to and pay tribute to a stronger power while keeping some of their own authority and daily life intact, a status that recurs throughout the semester, from Jehu of Israel under Assyria to Cyrus himself as a young Persian under Median rule before his revolt.
vizier (Week 5)
a high royal official in ancient Egypt, the role held by Ptahhotep, whose instructions to his son are framed as advice from an aging official of this rank.
W
wanax (Week 8)
the ruler at the head of a Mycenaean palace center's bureaucratic hierarchy of officials and scribes.
wheel, the (Week 2)
a technology first used in Mesopotamia for pottery-making around 3500 BC and only later adapted for carts, one of two key tools of this period that reshaped how quickly goods and people could move.
wrath (Week 16)
the anger of Achilles, named in the Iliad's very first word in the original Greek, which the poem says brought countless sorrows upon the Greeks.
Z
Zagros Mountains (Week 14)
the rugged highland region east of Mesopotamia where the Medes settled.
zebu (Week 7)
a distinctive local species of cattle domesticated in the Indus Valley region during its early farming period.
ziggurat (Week 2)
a massive stepped platform of mudbrick built to raise a temple, or god's house, above the ordinary buildings of a Sumerian city like Uruk.
