💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
No single English-language primary source excerpt from Olmec civilization exists in the way the Code of Hammurabi or the Narmer Palette's inscriptions do, since the Olmec left no deciphered texts of any length. The passage below is an adapted classroom account, written for this course, summarizing well-established archaeological findings about San Lorenzo and La Venta. Where the archaeological record is genuinely unresolved, the passage says so directly rather than manufacturing certainty.
📖 A CIVILIZATION ON THE GULF COAST

Along the humid, tropical lowlands where southern Mexico meets the Gulf of Mexico, a civilization historians call the Olmec built the earliest large-scale society anywhere in the Americas. Its first major center, San Lorenzo, flourished from roughly 1200 to 900 BC, the very period this course has spent studying Egypt's New Kingdom and the palace societies of the Aegean. When San Lorenzo declined, a second center, La Venta, rose in its place and flourished until roughly 400 BC.

Like Sumer, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and Shang China, the Olmec built their civilization beside rivers, in this case the Coatzacoalcos river system. But the resemblance has limits. Mesopotamia's farmers depended on constructed irrigation canals, dug, cleaned, and shared out among many households, to water a landscape that would otherwise be closer to desert. Olmec farmers faced a different problem entirely: the Coatzacoalcos region already received heavy seasonal rainfall and natural river flooding that deposited fresh, fertile soil across the floodplain every year, much like the Nile. No civilization-scale canal network has been found at San Lorenzo or La Venta. Whatever coordinated labor the Olmec did organize, it appears to have gone toward other projects instead.

The clearest of those projects are seventeen colossal stone heads, carved from single basalt boulders weighing as much as twenty tons and hauled, most likely by raft and log rollers, from a volcanic mountain range dozens of miles away. Each head has distinct, individual facial features, and most archaeologists believe they are portraits of specific rulers, since moving stone this size for anything other than a ruler's own commissioned monument seems unlikely. And yet not one of the seventeen heads carries a name. Whoever these rulers were, the Olmec left no inscription telling us who.

At La Venta, Olmec builders raised an earthen mound over a hundred feet tall, one of the largest single structures built anywhere in the Americas up to that time, arranged at the center of a complex of plazas and smaller platforms. Burials uncovered near these structures contained finely carved jade ornaments and other costly goods, while ordinary house sites nearby held far simpler belongings, evidence that Olmec society sorted itself into distinct ranks the way Sumer's and Egypt's societies did. Jade and other prized materials found at Olmec sites did not occur naturally nearby, meaning Olmec centers were connected to long-distance exchange networks reaching across a wide stretch of Mesoamerica.

One more piece of evidence remains genuinely disputed. In 1999, workers near San Lorenzo uncovered a flat stone slab, now called the Cascajal Block, carved with sixty-two symbols arranged in rows. Some archaeologists argue this is the oldest writing system yet found anywhere in the Western Hemisphere, dating to around 900 BC. Others point out that the block was not found in a scientifically controlled excavation, that no other object anything like it has ever turned up, and that scholars still cannot agree whether its symbols represent a true written language or something closer to a set of religious or decorative signs. As with the Indus Valley's undeciphered script, the honest answer is that no one knows for certain, and a careful historian says so rather than settling the question by assertion.
