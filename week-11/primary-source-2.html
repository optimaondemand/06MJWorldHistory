💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the inscriptions accompanying the carved registers of the Black Obelisk of Shalmaneser III, erected at Nimrud around 825 BC to record tribute received from several foreign rulers, including one register depicting Jehu of Israel.
📖 THE OBELISK

The inscription accompanying the register showing Jehu's submission:
"Tribute of Jehu, of the house of Omri: I received from him silver, gold, a golden bowl, a golden vase with a pointed base, golden tumblers, golden buckets, tin, a staff fit for a king, and shafts for spears."

A general statement of the obelisk's overall purpose, drawn from its opening lines:
"I have set up this monument to record the tribute of the kings of the lands, that all who come after may see the greatness of my reign and the submission of every ruler who stood before me."

A description of the accompanying carved images (an art-historical note, not a direct translation):
The register showing Jehu depicts him prostrate, face to the ground, before the Assyrian king, while a line of attendants behind him carries the tribute goods named in the inscription. It is one of the very few surviving ancient images of a specific, named Israelite ruler.

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE ON THE INSCRIPTION'S LANGUAGE
Assyrian scribes routinely described a defeated or tribute-paying kingdom's ruling line using the name of an earlier, often more famous dynasty, in this case "the house of Omri," a previous ruling family in the region, even though Jehu himself had ended that specific dynasty. This is a useful reminder that ancient inscriptions sometimes preserve administrative habits or shorthand that do not perfectly match a kingdom's own internal political history.
