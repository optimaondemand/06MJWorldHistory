📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What does a single carved image tell us that a whole page of writing cannot?

Ashurnasirpal II built the machine. His successors used it to reach further still. This lesson jumps forward roughly two decades, to the reign of his son Shalmaneser III (r. 859 to 824 BC), and to a single carved monument that captures, in unusually specific detail, the first direct point of contact between Assyria and the kingdoms this course will meet fully in Week 13.

📚 PART 1 · A MONUMENT OF TRIBUTE

The Black Obelisk, a tall, four-sided stone monument Shalmaneser III set up at Nimrud, records tribute received from five different foreign rulers, arranged in registers of small carved images running down its sides. Among them, one panel shows a bearded figure prostrating himself flat on the ground before the Assyrian king, while attendants carry silver, gold, and other precious goods. The accompanying inscription identifies this figure as Jehu, a king ruling in the region of Israel, and describes him presenting tribute, regular payments a lesser or defeated power sends to a stronger one to avoid conquest or continued attack.

This is a genuinely remarkable piece of evidence, for a reason worth naming plainly: it is one of the very few surviving images that any ancient Near Eastern artist made of a specific, named Israelite king. Everything else this course will read about the kingdoms of Israel and Judah in Week 13 comes from written text alone. Here, for one carved panel, a Mesopotamian sculptor has left behind an actual physical depiction of the political relationship between these two very different kingdoms, one enormous and expanding, the other small and increasingly caught between competing regional powers.

📚 PART 2 · VASSAL, NOT CONQUERED

Jehu's posture on the obelisk, prostrate, offering tribute rather than fighting, illustrates a status this course will see repeatedly across the next several weeks: a vassal, a ruler who keeps his own throne and his own people's daily life largely intact, in exchange for regular payment and acknowledged submission to a stronger power. This was, in its own way, a genuinely practical arrangement for a smaller kingdom facing an empire it had no realistic hope of defeating outright. Paying tribute preserved a kingdom's existence, even at the cost of its full independence. Whether that arrangement could last indefinitely, for Israel or for any of Assyria's other vassals, is a question this course will return to directly, since not every kingdom that becomes a vassal stays one for long.

📚 PART 3 · WHAT THIS SETS UP

This single carved image is a bridge, not a full story. Week 13 will tell the fuller history of the kingdoms of Israel and Judah, their own religious and political development, and their eventual fates under first Assyrian and then Babylonian power. For now, it is enough to notice that Assyria's expansion under Ashurnasirpal II and Shalmaneser III was not a distant, abstract process happening somewhere else. It reached directly into the same region, and touched the same peoples, this course will study in detail soon. Next week, this same Assyrian empire reaches its absolute height, under a king who built one of the ancient world's great libraries, before collapsing with startling speed.
