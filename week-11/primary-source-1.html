💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the royal annals of Ashurnasirpal II, carved to record his military campaigns during his reign (c. 883 to 859 BC). A content note: this text describes calculated violence against rebellious cities as deliberate state policy. It is presented here in restrained, adapted language, since the point of the lesson is to understand terror as a political strategy, not to dwell on graphic detail for its own sake.
📖 THE ANNALS

On the treatment of a city that had rebelled:
"I approached the city, and the terror of my dominion overwhelmed them. I captured the officials who had led the revolt. Many I flayed, and I covered a pillar at the city's gate with their skins, so that all who passed might see it. Others I impaled on stakes around the city, and some I walled up alive within its gates. In this way I struck fear into every land that heard of it."

On the results the king claims for this policy:
"After this, the surrounding lands heard of what I had done, and their hearts trembled. The kings of the neighboring cities came before me on their own, bringing tribute, silver, gold, and garments of fine wool, before I had so much as raised my weapon against them, for they had heard of my dealings with those who resisted."

On the king's building projects, presented as a mark of his glory:
"I built a great palace at Kalhu [Nimrud] for my royal dwelling, and I brought there cedar and cypress from the mountains, and skilled craftsmen from every conquered land, that they might work in my service and add to the splendor of my reign."

On a feast celebrating the new capital, recorded in unusually specific numbers:
"When I dedicated the palace at Kalhu, I invited many thousands of people from throughout my land and from among the peoples I had conquered, and for ten days I fed and entertained them all, that they might witness the greatness of my kingdom with their own eyes."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Historians read a text like this with two things in mind at once: it is very likely an accurate record of real events, since Assyrian kings had every reason to boast about their conquests rather than invent violence that had not happened, and it is also, quite deliberately, a piece of propaganda, designed to frighten anyone who might consider rebellion before it began.
