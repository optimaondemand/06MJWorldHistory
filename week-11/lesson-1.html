📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can fear alone build an empire that lasts?

Week 10 closed with Assyria strong enough to sack Thebes, deep in Egyptian territory, in 663 BC. This lesson goes back roughly two centuries earlier, to the reign of the king who did more than perhaps any other single ruler to build Assyria into the terrifying military power that made an achievement like that possible: Ashurnasirpal II, who ruled from roughly 883 to 859 BC.

📚 PART 1 · A VACUUM WAITING TO BE FILLED

After the Bronze Age Collapse this course studied in Week 9, Mesopotamia spent more than two centuries without any single dominant power, small rival kingdoms jostling for advantage in the region's north. Assyria itself had existed for a very long time, since well before Hammurabi's Babylon in Week 4, mostly as one regional power among several. Ashurnasirpal II changed that, rebuilding Assyria's army, moving his capital to a rebuilt city called Nimrud, and beginning the string of conquests that his successors would extend into what historians call the Neo-Assyrian Empire, eventually the largest empire the Near East had yet seen.

📚 PART 2 · TERROR BY DESIGN

This week's Primary Source, drawn from Ashurnasirpal II's own royal annals, records his campaigns in language that is, quite deliberately, meant to frighten. He describes flaying rebellious officials alive and displaying their skins on city walls, impaling captives on stakes outside a rebelling city's gates, and building towers from the skulls of defeated enemies, and he has these acts carved and publicly recorded rather than hidden. This is worth pausing on directly rather than looking away from. Ashurnasirpal was not simply cruel in some incidental, personal way. He was using publicized, calculated violence as a deliberate instrument of imperial policy, a warning broadcast to every city that might consider rebellion: submit, pay tribute, and live, or resist, and this is what will happen to you.

⚠️ A QUESTION WORTH ASKING HONESTLY
This course has repeatedly asked whether a people's laws, customs, and habits help explain their success or failure at first-order political problems: keeping internal peace, defending against outside threats, and binding a community together. Ashurnasirpal's terror clearly served the first two goals, at least for a time. Cities that had seen what happened to a rebellious neighbor thought twice before rising up themselves, and Assyria's borders expanded rapidly and held. But the third goal, binding a large, multi-ethnic empire into something like a shared political community, is a harder question, and this lesson will not answer it fully yet. Fear can win a battle and even secure a province, but whether fear alone can hold an empire together across generations, long after the king who inspired it is gone, is precisely the question Week 12 answers, when this same Assyrian empire, at the very height of its power, collapses far faster than anyone watching it at its peak might have expected.
📚 PART 3 · BUILDING THE MACHINE

Ashurnasirpal's terror was backed by a genuinely new kind of military institution. Earlier armies this course has studied, in Sumer, Akkad, or even New Kingdom Egypt, relied heavily on seasonal levies, farmers and townsmen called up for a campaign and then sent home again. Assyria built something closer to a standing, professional army, year-round soldiers equipped increasingly with iron weapons rather than bronze, supported by dedicated engineering units skilled at building siege towers and battering rams to break through even well-defended city walls, and eventually large, disciplined cavalry units as well. This professional military machine, built and expanded across nearly two centuries of Assyrian kings after Ashurnasirpal, is what allowed Assyria's later rulers to reach as far as Egypt itself.
