💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
The Instructions of Ptahhotep is one of the oldest surviving wisdom texts in the world, traditionally attributed to Ptahhotep, a vizier serving under an Old Kingdom pharaoh, and framed as advice to his son. The maxims below are adapted into plain modern English from early public domain English translations of the Egyptian text (based on the Prisse Papyrus, itself a later copy of an older original) to preserve their meaning and structure while making the language accessible to a modern student.
📖 THE MAXIMS

On humility, whatever your position:
"Do not be arrogant because of your knowledge. Take counsel with the ignorant as well as the wise, for the limits of skill can never be reached, and no one born is already perfect in judgment. Good speech is more hidden than the precious emerald, and yet it may be found even among servant girls grinding grain at the millstone."

On the danger of a hasty temper:
"If you meet an opponent in argument who is your equal, arguing on ground where you stand equally matched, prove yourself greater than he by silence, even while he speaks badly, until those who hear him say, 'How much finer is the one who does not answer than the one who provoked him.'"

On a leader's responsibility to those beneath him:
"If you are placed in authority over others, seek out every good and useful act, that your conduct may be without fault. Great is justice, and its rightness endures; it has not been shaken since the time of its founder, while the one who transgresses its laws is punished. Wrongdoing may seize riches for a time, but wickedness never lands its cargo safely in the end."

On listening to a petitioner without contempt:
"If you are a man who leads, one to whom others bring their petitions, listen calmly to what a petitioner has to say. Do not rebuff him before he has unburdened himself of what he meant to say. A man who has suffered wrong wants his heart to be eased even more than he wants his case to succeed."

On self-government within one's own household:
"If you prosper and found your own household, love your wife at home as is fitting. Fill her belly, clothe her back; these are the ways to care for her body. Gladden her heart as long as you live, for she is a profitable field for her lord."

On avoiding the vice of greed:
"Guard against the vice of greed, a grievous sickness with no cure. It divides fathers from sons, and mothers from the children they bore. It strips a man of those who serve him faithfully. Greed shrinks the great man who was full of it down to nothing at all."
