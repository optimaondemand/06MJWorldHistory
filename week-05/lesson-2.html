📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Who really built the Great Pyramid, and what did an all-powerful pharaoh's own officials teach about virtue?

Roughly five centuries after Narmer's unification, Egypt entered the period historians call the Old Kingdom (c. 2686 to 2181 BC), an era of strong centralized pharaonic rule and the most ambitious building projects the ancient world had yet attempted. This lesson looks at the Old Kingdom's most famous monument, what building it actually required, and a very different kind of Egyptian text: not a king's boast, but practical advice for living well.

📚 PART 1 · THE GREAT PYRAMID

Around 2560 BC, under the pharaoh Khufu, workers completed the Great Pyramid of Giza, originally rising about 481 feet, built from over two million limestone and granite blocks, some weighing several tons apiece. For centuries, popular imagination pictured this as the work of masses of enslaved laborers driven by the whip. Archaeological evidence recovered from workers' villages near the pyramids tells a different story. Excavated bakeries, breweries, and cattle bones show that the pyramid workforce was fed a substantial diet including beer and meat, was housed in organized barracks, and appears to have included skilled permanent craftsmen alongside rotating gangs of Egyptian farmers, likely conscripted for a set season each year, probably during the months when the Nile flood made fieldwork impossible anyway. This does not mean the labor was easy or freely chosen in the way a modern job is. But it was organized, provisioned, state labor, not simply mass slavery, and it is worth correcting this popular misconception plainly rather than letting it stand.

📚 PART 2 · PTAHHOTEP'S ADVICE

Not every Old Kingdom text is a royal monument. The Instructions of Ptahhotep, one of the oldest surviving wisdom texts from anywhere in the ancient world, is framed as advice from an aging vizier, a high royal official, to his son and successor. Rather than boasting of conquest or divine favor, Ptahhotep's advice concerns everyday virtue: how to listen well, how a powerful man should treat those beneath him without arrogance, how to control one's temper, and how to recognize that wisdom can come from anyone, regardless of rank. This week's primary source lets you read some of that advice directly. It is worth noticing how different this text feels from a pharaoh's monumental inscription. Where Narmer's Palette and a pyramid's scale project power outward, Ptahhotep's instructions look inward, at the private, personal virtues, courtesy, self-control, patience, that Egyptian culture expected even from its most powerful men.

⚠️ DON'T FLATTEN THIS
It is tempting to picture the pyramids as either pure oppression or a straightforward national achievement everyone happily volunteered for. Resist that. The labor was real, organized, and almost certainly compulsory for most rotating workers, even as it was fed, housed, and skillfully managed rather than driven by mass slavery. Correcting a myth is not the same thing as declaring the reality effortless or freely chosen.
📚 PART 3 · DIVINE KINGSHIP, HELD IN TENSION

Taken together, this week's two lessons present a pharaoh who was, on one hand, treated as a living god responsible for maintaining ma'at across the entire cosmos, and on the other hand, a ruler whose own officials wrote guidance on ordinary human virtues like patience and self-restraint. These are not necessarily in contradiction. A king who is understood to embody cosmic order might be expected, precisely because of that role, to model the personal order and self-government that order requires at every level, from the pharaoh's own household down to the humblest farmer's. Compare this again to Mesopotamia: Hammurabi justified his kingship by a promise of justice enforced through law; Egypt's Old Kingdom pharaoh justified his rule through a claimed identity with the divine itself, reinforced, this lesson suggests, by an expectation of personal virtue rather than law alone. Keep watching for how this claim to divine kingship holds up, or comes under strain, as Egypt's story continues in the weeks ahead.
