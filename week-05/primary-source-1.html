The Narmer Palette (Inscriptions and Scenes)
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
The Narmer Palette is a ceremonial siltstone slab, about 25 inches tall, discovered at Hierakonpolis in Upper Egypt in 1898. Unlike the Code of Hammurabi, it carries almost no continuous written text. Egyptian hieroglyphic writing was still in its earliest form around 3100 BC, mostly limited to names and short labels rather than full sentences. What the Palette does carry is a small number of hieroglyphic captions alongside carved scenes arranged in horizontal registers, or bands, on both its front and back faces. The description below identifies what is carved on each register and which readings historians consider secure versus debated.
📖 THE PALETTE, REGISTER BY REGISTER

Both faces, top register. A rectangular frame known as a serekh, representing a palace facade, appears at the top of both sides. Inside it are two hieroglyphs, a catfish and a chisel, which Egyptologists read as the name Nar-mer. This reading is considered secure and is the reason the Palette and the king are both known by this name today. On either side of the serekh is a human face with cow's horns and ears, identified with the goddess Hathor or a related cow-deity called Bat.

Back of the Palette, second register. A large figure, taller than all others on the Palette (a common way Egyptian art marked importance), wears the White Crown of Upper Egypt and grips a kneeling captive by the hair, mace raised above him in a pose of ritual execution. Beside the captive's head are two hieroglyphs, a harpoon and a rectangular sign generally read as a word for land or marsh region, which most historians interpret as the name of a place or people the king has conquered, most likely somewhere in the Nile Delta or its marshy fringe. A smaller attendant figure, carrying the king's sandals, follows behind him. Above the captive floats a falcon, identified with the god Horus, perched on a clump of papyrus plants (papyrus was strongly associated with Lower Egypt) and holding a rope tied through the nose of a human head that emerges from the same papyrus clump, another image widely read as a personification of conquered Lower Egyptian territory.

Back of the Palette, bottom register. Two fallen, apparently dead, enemies lie beneath the king's feet, shown from above, one with a hieroglyphic sign beside him sometimes read as a place name and sometimes left untranslated. Historians disagree about whether every caption on this register can be confidently read at all.

Front of the Palette, top register (below the serekh). The king appears again, now wearing the Red Crown of Lower Egypt, walking in procession behind a standard-bearer, toward ten decapitated enemies laid out in two rows, each with his severed head placed between his legs.

Front of the Palette, middle register. Two long-necked mythical animals, sometimes called serpopards, have their intertwined necks forming a circular depression at the center of the stone, which may have been used to grind cosmetic pigment, since palettes of this general type were originally a common Egyptian household object before being adapted for royal ceremonial use.

Front of the Palette, bottom register. A bull, a symbol of royal strength used elsewhere in Egyptian art for the king himself, tramples a fortified settlement and gores a fallen enemy, its walls shown from above.

💡 A NOTE ON INTERPRETATION
Some historians read the Palette as a documentary record of one specific, historical military campaign that achieved Egypt's unification. Others argue it is closer to a formal, symbolic statement of the king's role as unifier and conqueror, created to celebrate a process of consolidation that may have unfolded over a longer period, or through more than one campaign, compressed here into a single ceremonial image. Both readings agree the Palette marks, or at least commemorates, the joining of Upper and Lower Egypt under one crown.
