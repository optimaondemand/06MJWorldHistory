Ashurbanipal's Library Tablets
Primary Source Reading
💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of Ashurbanipal's own inscriptions describing his education and his library at Nineveh, along with a representative scribal colophon of the kind attached to many tablets in the collection.
📖 THE INSCRIPTIONS

On his own education as a young prince (unusual for a Mesopotamian king):
"I, Ashurbanipal, learned within the palace the wisdom of Nabu, the whole of the scribe's craft. I studied the heavens and the earth with the masters of the scholars. I can read the cunning tablets of Sumer and the obscure Akkadian, hard to master. I have examined inscriptions on stone from before the flood. I hold in my heart the wisdom of Adapa, the hidden secret of the scribal art."

On gathering the library's contents:
"I commanded that tablets be sought out in every city of my kingdom, wherever learned writings could be found, and brought to my palace at Nineveh, both the originals and copies made by my own scribes, that nothing of the ancient wisdom of Sumer and Akkad should be lost."

A typical scribal colophon attached to a tablet in the collection:
"Palace of Ashurbanipal, king of the world, king of Assyria, who trusts in the gods Ashur and Ninlil. I have written down the wisdom of the ancients upon this tablet according to the originals, and set it within my palace for my own instruction. Whoever removes this tablet or writes his own name upon it in place of mine, may Ashur and Ninlil overthrow him in fury and destroy his name and his seed from the land."

On the library's contents, drawn from what has actually been recovered:
Among the many thousands of tablets recovered from Nineveh are a nearly complete copy of the Epic of Gilgamesh, astronomical and medical texts, dictionaries pairing Sumerian and Akkadian words, and administrative and legal records, an enormous cross-section of Mesopotamian scholarly knowledge gathered under a single royal roof.

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
The library's clay tablets, unbaked when Nineveh's palace was destroyed by fire in 612 BC, were accidentally hardened by that same fire, a detail explored directly in this week's graded VR activity.
