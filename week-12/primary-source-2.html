The Babylonian Chronicle (on the Fall of Nineveh, 612 BC)

💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the Babylonian Chronicle covering the fall of Nineveh, a terse, year-by-year record typical of Mesopotamian chronicle writing, listing events with little commentary or explanation.
📖 THE CHRONICLE

On the alliance against Assyria:
"In that year, the king of Babylon called out his army and marched forth. The king of the Medes also called out his army and marched to join the king of Babylon. They met one another, and their armies moved together against the land of Assyria."

On the siege of Nineveh:
"They encamped against Nineveh. From the month of Simanu until the month of Abu, for three months, they pressed the siege against the city. On the appointed day, they inflicted a great defeat upon the people. At that time, Sin-shar-ishkun, king of Assyria, [the text is damaged at this point, and its exact meaning is uncertain]."

On the city's capture:
"They captured the city and turned it into ruin heaps. They carried off a great quantity of spoil from the city and from the temple, and they made the city into a mound and a ruin."

On the aftermath, in the chronicle's characteristically terse style:
"The king of Babylon and the king of the Medes returned to their own lands after these events. In this way was Assyria brought to its end."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE ON THIS CHRONICLE'S STYLE
Notice how little explanation or emotion this text offers, even while describing the fall of one of the ancient world's largest empires. Babylonian chronicles were kept as factual, year-by-year administrative records, not as dramatic narratives, which is precisely why historians treat them as valuable but also why they leave so many specific details, including the exact fate of Nineveh's last king, genuinely uncertain.
