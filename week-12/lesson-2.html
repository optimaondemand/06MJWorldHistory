📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can a civilization look unstoppable and still fail its one true test?

Ashurbanipal died around 627 BC, ruler of the largest empire the Near East had yet seen. Within fifteen years, that empire's capital had burned, and within a generation, the Neo-Assyrian Empire itself had ceased to exist entirely. This is one of the most sudden reversals of fortune this course will study, and it deserves to be taken as seriously as Assyria's rise was in Week 11.

📚 PART 1 · A FAST, UGLY SUCCESSION

Ashurbanipal's death was followed quickly by a succession crisis among his own sons and rival claimants, weakening the central authority that had held Assyria's vast, multi-ethnic empire together. This is exactly the kind of internal political failure Week 11 flagged as an open question: could terror alone bind together the many different peoples, cities, and former kingdoms Assyria had conquered, once the strong king who had inspired that terror was gone? The answer, playing out within just a few years of Ashurbanipal's death, was no. Vassal kingdoms and rival powers alike sensed weakness, and two of them, Babylon to the south and the Medes from the Zagros Mountains to the east, a people this course will meet in full in Week 14, formed an alliance against their common former master.

📚 PART 2 · THE FALL OF NINEVEH

This week's second Primary Source, drawn from a Babylonian chronicle, records the campaign in the terse, factual style typical of Mesopotamian chronicles, listing marches, sieges, and outcomes with almost no editorializing at all. In 612 BC, the combined Babylonian-Median army besieged Nineveh itself, Ashurbanipal's great capital and the home of his library, for roughly three months before the city fell. The Assyrian king at the time, Sin-shar-ishkun, is recorded as dying during the city's fall, though the chronicle's terse style leaves some of the exact details genuinely uncertain. Nineveh was thoroughly destroyed and never rebuilt as an imperial capital, a fate the Hittite Empire had already suffered after the Bronze Age Collapse in Week 9, though on a far larger scale and after a far shorter warning.

⚠️ A QUESTION WORTH ANSWERING DIRECTLY
This course has repeatedly asked whether a people's laws, customs, and habits explain their success or failure at first-order political problems, especially the capacity to organize against external threats. War is, in a real sense, the ultimate test of that capacity, since it is the one problem an empire cannot fake its way through with propaganda alone. Assyria, at the height of its power under Ashurbanipal, looked as though it could not possibly fail that test. A single generation later, it failed completely, collapsing faster than the more gradual Egyptian decline this course studied in Week 10. Terror had won Assyria an empire. It did not, it turns out, leave behind the kind of shared political community, the bonds of loyalty and shared purpose, that might have held that empire together once its strongest king was gone.
📚 PART 3 · WHAT COMES NEXT

Nineveh's fall did not end Mesopotamian empire itself, only the Assyrian one. Babylon, one of the two victorious powers in this campaign, was about to enter its own most famous and powerful period, under a king whose building projects would become legendary. Week 13 turns there directly.
