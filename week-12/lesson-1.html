📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What does an empire's greatest king choose to leave behind?

Assyria's empire, built through Ashurnasirpal II's terror and Shalmaneser III's steady expansion in Week 11, reached its widest extent and its greatest cultural achievement under one final king: Ashurbanipal, who ruled from roughly 668 to 627 BC. Under him, Assyria stretched from the Persian Gulf to the Nile, briefly ruling Egypt itself after the sack of Thebes this course met in Week 10. But Ashurbanipal's most lasting achievement was not a conquest at all.

📚 PART 1 · A KING WHO LEARNED TO READ

Assyrian kings were warriors first, and Ashurbanipal was no exception, boasting in his own inscriptions of hunting lions and leading campaigns. But he also claims something highly unusual for a Mesopotamian king: that he was personally taught to read and write cuneiform as a young prince, a skill normally left entirely to trained scribes. In his own words, adapted in this week's Primary Source, he describes learning "the wisdom of Nabu," the Mesopotamian god of writing and knowledge, studying difficult scholarly tablets in both Akkadian and the much older Sumerian language, a language that had not been spoken for well over a thousand years by his time but was still preserved, studied, and copied by scribes the way this course might treat a genuinely ancient classical language today.

📚 PART 2 · THE LIBRARY AT NINEVEH

Ashurbanipal used his position, and his empire's resources, to gather an enormous royal library at his capital, Nineveh, sending agents throughout Assyria and especially into Babylonia to collect, copy, and bring back tablets on every subject: medicine, astronomy, omens, grammar, royal history, and literature. Among the many thousands of tablets recovered from this library by modern archaeologists is a nearly complete copy of the Epic of Gilgamesh, the same flood story and heroic epic this course first read all the way back in Week 2, preserved here in its fullest surviving ancient form specifically because Ashurbanipal's scribes thought it worth copying and keeping. Without this library, much of what this course, and modern scholarship generally, knows about Sumerian and Babylonian literature might simply be lost.

Ashurbanipal's tablets often end with a colophon, a short scribal note identifying the tablet's owner and sometimes warning future readers against removing or damaging it, occasionally invoking the gods' anger against anyone who might steal or destroy the collection. It is a small, very human detail: a king proud enough of his library to threaten curses on its behalf.

⚠️ AN IRONY WORTH SITTING WITH
Here is a detail worth pausing on directly, since it connects this week's two lessons together. Nineveh's palace, library included, would be destroyed by fire within a generation of Ashurbanipal's death, a disaster Lesson 2 describes in full. Fire, under most circumstances, destroys writing. But clay tablets behave differently than papyrus or parchment. The very fire that destroyed Ashurbanipal's palace baked his unbaked clay tablets hard, in effect firing them like pottery, and in doing so helped preserve thousands of them, shattered but largely intact, for archaeologists to recover and reassemble more than two thousand years later. A catastrophe that ended a king's dynasty accidentally safeguarded some of his greatest achievement. History rarely hands out its ironies this cleanly, but this one is genuine, and it is exactly the kind of detail this week's graded VR activity asks you to sit with directly.
