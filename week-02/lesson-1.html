📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why did people choose to depend on strangers?

By around 3450 BC, in the marshy floodplain where the Tigris and Euphrates rivers run down toward the Persian Gulf, a settlement called Uruk had grown into something the world had never quite seen before: a true city. Not a large village, not a cluster of farming hamlets, but a single, dense, walled community that may have held as many as forty thousand people at its height, ringed by a wall some historians credit to its legendary king Gilgamesh. To understand why this mattered, and why it happened here first, you need to understand both the region, called Sumer, and the pressures that pushed people to live packed together at a scale no Neolithic village ever attempted.

📚 PART 1 · A LANDSCAPE THAT DEMANDED COOPERATION

Southern Mesopotamia is not naturally generous land. Rainfall is scarce, and without irrigation, the region is closer to desert than farmland. But the Tigris and Euphrates carry meltwater down from distant mountains every year, and the silt they deposit makes the soil, once watered, extraordinarily fertile. The catch is that no single family can dig, maintain, and manage a network of irrigation canals alone. Canals have to be planned, dug, cleaned of silt, and shared out fairly across many households at once, or the whole system fails and everyone's harvest suffers together.

This is a genuine first-order economic problem: how do you organize enough people, reliably enough, to keep a shared irrigation system working year after year? Sumer's answer was the city-state, a single city and its surrounding farmland, governed as one unit, capable of mustering labor and resources at a scale no individual village could match.

📚 PART 2 · TEMPLES, PRIESTS, AND THE FIRST BUREAUCRATS

At the center of Uruk stood a great temple complex eventually built up into a ziggurat, a massive stepped platform of mudbrick meant to raise the god's house above the ordinary buildings of the city. The temple was not merely a religious center. It functioned as Uruk's central storehouse and redistribution hub: farmers delivered a portion of their grain, wool, and livestock to the temple, and temple administrators, called en or ensi depending on the era and city, tracked what came in and directed how it was given back out, to laborers, craftsmen, and priests. This arrangement, part religious devotion, part economic administration, meant that Uruk's earliest rulers were as much accountants as they were kings.

Managing this much incoming and outgoing grain by memory alone becomes impossible past a certain scale, and this practical problem is what drove Sumerians toward writing, a story Lesson 2 takes up directly. It also drove something else historians consider a hallmark of true civilization: specialized labor and social class. In a farming village, nearly everyone farms. In a city like Uruk, the temple's surplus could support people who did nothing but make pottery, weave cloth, cut stone, or administer records, full time. Society sorted itself into ranks: priestly administrators at the top, skilled craftsmen and merchants in the middle, laborers and farmers below them. This sorting is one of the clearest markers historians use to identify a true civilization rather than simply a large village: dense population, monumental architecture, specialized labor, social stratification, and (soon) writing, all appearing together for the first time at Uruk.

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW · TWO NEW TOOLS OF THE AGE
The wheel, first used for pottery-making and only later adapted for carts, appears in Mesopotamia by roughly 3500 BC. And working alongside older stone tools, Sumerians increasingly turned to metal: copper, worked since the Ubaid period that came just before Uruk's rise, and eventually bronze, an alloy of copper and tin that held a sharper edge and helped push the region fully into what archaeologists call the Bronze Age by around 3000 BC. This is the fullest treatment either thread gets in this course; watch for how later civilizations put both to new uses.
📚 PART 3 · A NEW KIND OF QUESTION

This week's Discussion Topic asks "Why Cities?" It's worth sitting with the fact that cities are not simply villages that grew large by accident. A city like Uruk represents thousands of individual households choosing, generation after generation, to depend on strangers, on a shared canal system, a shared temple storehouse, a shared set of administrators, rather than on their own extended family alone. That is a genuine gamble, and Sumer's temple economy was one particular, historically contingent answer to it, not the only possible one, as you will see when this course turns to Egypt in Week 5.
