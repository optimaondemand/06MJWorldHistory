📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do you remember a number too large to hold in your head?

Lesson 1 ended with a practical problem: how do you keep track of a temple's grain, wool, and livestock once the numbers grow too large to hold in memory? Sumer's answer, developed gradually over centuries, became one of the most consequential inventions in human history.

📚 PART 1 · FROM TOKENS TO TRUE WRITING

Long before anyone in Mesopotamia wrote a sentence, temple administrators used small clay tokens, shaped like cones, spheres, and disks, to represent quantities of grain, oil, or animals. By around 3300 BC, administrators began pressing these tokens into the surface of a wet clay tablet to leave a record, and then, more efficiently still, simply drawing the token's shape directly into the clay with a reed stylus. Over the following centuries, these pictures grew more abstract, more standardized, and eventually more capable of representing sounds as well as objects, becoming true writing. Because the wedge-shaped marks left by a reed stylus pressed into clay give this script its name, we call it cuneiform, from the Latin for "wedge-shaped." A person trained to read and write it, a scribe, became one of the most valuable and respected professions in Mesopotamian society, and scribal schools trained new generations to keep the empire of records running.

Notice how directly this connects to Lesson 1's temple economy. Writing in Sumer was not invented to record poetry or philosophy first. Its earliest, overwhelming use was administrative: how many sheep, how much barley, delivered by whom, owed to whom. The literature and history writing that cuneiform would eventually make possible, including this lesson's second half, came later, riding on top of a system built first to solve an accounting problem.

📚 PART 2 · KINGSHIP DESCENDED FROM HEAVEN

Once cuneiform matured into a flexible tool, Sumerian scribes began recording their own past, and one of the most fascinating documents to survive is the Sumerian King List, a long record of cities and kings stretching back, in the scribes' own telling, before a great flood swept over the land. According to the list, "kingship descended from heaven" and was first held by the city of Eridu, then passed from city to city among a handful of legendary rulers who reigned for what the list records as tens of thousands of years each, before the flood came and swept everything away. After the flood, the list says, kingship descended from heaven a second time, this time to the city of Kish, and from there the reigns recorded shrink down toward more plausible, historical lengths.

What should a careful reader make of this? The King List mixes what looks like genuine historical memory (real cities, in something close to a real order of political dominance) with what is clearly legend (impossibly long reigns, a single catastrophic flood ending an entire age). This is not unique to Sumer. Cultures across the ancient world tend to remember their own deep past through story and legend before they remember it through anything resembling careful record-keeping, and separating the two threads honestly, without dismissing either, is one of the central skills of historical thinking. Gilgamesh himself, the king credited with building Uruk's wall, appears on the King List as a real, historical ruler of Uruk's dynasty, yet he is also the hero of the Epic of Gilgamesh, an epic poem built up over centuries that gives him journeys, battles with monsters, and a quest for eternal life that no historian treats as literal fact. A single figure, in other words, can be both a real king remembered in an administrative document and a legendary hero remembered in poetry, at the very same time.

⚠️ DON'T MIX THESE UP
A source can contain both real historical memory and legend at once. Don't assume that finding one impossible detail (a 28,800-year reign) means the whole document is worthless, and don't assume that one plausible-sounding detail (a real city name) proves every detail is literal fact. Read for both threads at once.
📚 PART 3 · READING THE FLOOD STORY DIRECTLY

This week's primary sources let you read both threads for yourself. Primary Source 1 is an excerpt from the Epic of Gilgamesh's own flood story, told to Gilgamesh by a survivor named Utnapishtim, one of the oldest flood narratives ever recorded, and a text worth comparing honestly to other flood stories you may already know rather than treating any single version as simply "the same story" repeated. Primary Source 2 is an excerpt from the Sumerian King List itself, including its account of the flood and the return of kingship afterward. Read each on its own terms, and ask, as you go, which parts feel like memory and which feel like legend, and why that distinction matters.
