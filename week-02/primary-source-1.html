💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Excerpted and lightly adapted from public-domain scholarly translations of Tablet XI of the Standard Babylonian Epic of Gilgamesh. In this scene, Utnapishtim, who survived a great flood sent by the gods and was granted eternal life, tells his story to the hero Gilgamesh, who has traveled to the edge of the world seeking the secret of immortality.
📖 THE TEXT

Utnapishtim said to Gilgamesh:

"I will reveal to you a hidden thing, Gilgamesh, a secret of the gods I will tell you. You know the city Shuruppak, a city that lies on the bank of the Euphrates. That city was old, and the gods within it had come to a decision. The great gods resolved to send a flood, all of them: Anu their father, valiant Enlil their counsellor, Ninurta their helper, Ennugi their keeper of canals. And Ea, the clever one, was sworn to secrecy with them, yet he repeated their plan to a reed fence.

"'Reed fence, reed fence! Wall, wall! Listen, reed fence, and pay attention, wall: Man of Shuruppak, tear down your house, build a boat! Abandon your possessions, seek life instead of riches, save yourself from what is coming! Take aboard the boat the seed of all living things. The boat you are to build, let her measurements be measured out: let her width and length be equal, and roof her over as the ocean below is roofed by the sky.'

"I understood, and said to Ea my lord, 'I will do as you have commanded. But what shall I say to the city, the people, and the elders?' And Ea gave me the words to speak, so I would not be suspected of betraying the gods' plan.

"I gathered all my kin and had them help me. I built the boat, six decks in all, dividing it into compartments. I caulked it with pitch and bitumen. I loaded aboard all that I had of silver and gold, and the seed of all living things, my family and kin, the beasts of the field, the wild creatures, and every skilled craftsman I could gather. When the appointed time came, one who sends rain at dusk sent a black cloud, and I watched the storm gathering and grew afraid, and went aboard the boat and sealed the door behind me.

"For six days and six nights the wind blew, the flood-storm flattened the land. When the seventh day arrived, the storm and flood-wave stopped, which had struggled like a woman in labor. The sea grew calm, the tempest grew still, the flood ceased. I looked at the weather, and stillness had set in, and all mankind had turned back into clay. The landscape was flat as a rooftop. I opened a hatch, and light fell on my face. I fell to my knees, sat down, and wept, tears running down my face.

"I looked for coastlines in the expanse of the sea, and at twelve leagues distance there emerged a region of land. On Mount Nisir the boat came to a halt. Mount Nisir held the boat fast and did not let it move. One day, a second day, Mount Nisir held the boat fast. A third day, a fourth day, Mount Nisir held the boat fast. A fifth day, a sixth day, Mount Nisir held the boat fast.

"When the seventh day arrived, I sent forth a dove and set it free. The dove went off, but came back to me, since no perching place was visible to it. Then I sent forth a swallow and set it free. The swallow went off, but came back to me, since no perching place was visible to it. Then I sent forth a raven and set it free. The raven went off, and it saw the waters receding. It ate, circled, left droppings, and did not come back.

"Then I sent out everything in all directions and sacrificed a sheep. I offered incense in front of the mountain-ziggurat. The gods smelled the sweet savor and gathered like flies over the sacrifice."
