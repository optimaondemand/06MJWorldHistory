💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from scholarly translations of the Behistun Inscription, carved at Darius I's command on a cliff face at Mount Behistun in three languages: Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian.
📖 THE BEHISTUN INSCRIPTION

On Darius's claim to the throne:
"I am Darius, the great king, king of kings, king of Persia... By the favor of Ahura Mazda, I am king. A man named Gaumata, a Magian, rose up and lied to the people, saying, 'I am Bardiya, son of Cyrus, brother of Cambyses.' Then all the people turned from Cambyses and went over to him."

On exposing the impostor:
"I say that this Gaumata the Magian lied. The real Bardiya had been killed by Cambyses before this. I, with a few loyal men, slew Gaumata the Magian and those who were his foremost followers, and I took the kingdom that had belonged to my family."

On the rebellions that followed:
"After I became king, there were provinces that rebelled against me, and in each land men lied and rose up. By the grace of Ahura Mazda, I fought nine battles and defeated them, and I took captive the men who called themselves kings in these lands."

On the inscription's own purpose:
"I have caused this inscription to be written in three languages, so that whoever reads it hereafter may know these things as they truly happened, and may not believe the lies of any who would say otherwise."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Darius's account is the only detailed surviving record of these events, carved at his own command to justify his seizure of the throne, and no independent source exists to confirm his claim that Gaumata was truly an impostor rather than the real Bardiya. The inscription's true significance for this course lies less in settling that question than in its trilingual form: because the same text appears in Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian, it became the essential key nineteenth-century scholars used to decipher cuneiform writing, the same script behind nearly every Mesopotamian Primary Source this course has studied this semester.
