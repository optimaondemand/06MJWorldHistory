💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
Adapted into plain modern English from public domain English translations of Herodotus's Histories, Book III, describing Cambyses's conquest of Egypt and his conduct afterward.
📖 THE HISTORIES, BOOK III

On the victory at Pelusium:
"Cambyses led his army against Egypt, and at Pelusium met the forces of Psamtik, son of Amasis, in battle. The Egyptians, though they fought bravely, were defeated and driven back, and Egypt soon after passed entirely into Persian hands."

On the treatment of the Apis bull:
"When Cambyses saw the Egyptians rejoicing at the appearance of Apis, the sacred bull, he grew enraged, believing they mocked his own recent misfortunes, and in his anger he wounded the animal, an act the Egyptians considered a grave sacrilege against their gods."

On accusations of instability:
"From this time onward, those who were with him say that Cambyses became openly deranged, committing acts of violence against his own family and household that he would surely have avoided in a sound state of mind."

📝 EDITORIAL NOTE
Herodotus wrote this account roughly a century after Cambyses's reign, and much of his information likely came from Egyptian sources with strong reasons to resent the Persian conqueror who had just ended their native dynasty. No Persian account of Cambyses's own reign, comparable to the Cyrus Cylinder or the Behistun Inscription, survives to weigh against this hostile portrait. This does not mean every detail is false, but it does mean this account should be read as one side of a story this course cannot fully verify, not as a neutral record of Cambyses's character.
