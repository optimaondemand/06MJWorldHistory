📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can a single battle really mark the end of one civilization's independence and the start of something the ancient world had never quite seen?

Cyrus the Great died in 530 BC, and his son Cambyses II inherited an empire already stretching from Anatolia to the edge of Mesopotamia. Cambyses did not wait long to expand it further, turning almost immediately toward the one great river valley civilization this course has studied that Persia did not yet control.

📚 PART 1 · EGYPT FALLS AT PELUSIUM

By 525 BC, Egypt was ruled by Psamtik III, the latest king of the Saite Dynasty this course studied reviving Old Kingdom art and tradition back in Week 10. At the border fortress city of Pelusium, on the eastern edge of the Nile Delta, Cambyses's Persian army met and decisively defeated the Egyptian forces. Egypt, the civilization this course has followed since the Nile's predictable flooding and Narmer's unification in Week 5, became a Persian satrapy, a province governed under Persian oversight, its own long, independent royal tradition now formally at an end.

This moment deserves to be named directly and carefully. For the first time in the history this course has studied, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Persia's own homeland were bound together under a single ruler. Two of the ancient world's oldest and most storied civilizations, each with thousands of years of its own kings, gods, and written record behind it, now answered to the same distant court. This is not a small event to fold quietly into a larger story. It is, in a real sense, the arrival of something the ancient Near East had never quite seen before: one empire, spanning what had always been separate worlds.

📚 PART 2 · A PORTRAIT WORTH DOUBTING

This week's first Primary Source turns to Herodotus's account of Cambyses in his Histories, Book III, and it is worth reading with real caution, for reasons beyond the legendary uncertainty this course has already applied to Herodotus's stories of Deioces in Week 14 and Cyrus in Week 17. Herodotus's Cambyses is not merely a conqueror. He is portrayed as increasingly unstable and impious, accused of killing Egypt's sacred Apis bull out of contempt for Egyptian religion, mistreating the embalmed body of the previous pharaoh, and committing violent crimes against his own family back in Persia.

This course should ask a direct question of this account: what would a Persian source, describing the same king, most likely say instead? Honestly, this course cannot fully answer that question, because no surviving Persian inscription describing Cambyses's own reign and character in his own words has come down to us the way the Cyrus Cylinder or Darius's own inscriptions have. What this course can say is that Herodotus was writing generations later, as a Greek, likely drawing in part on Egyptian priestly informants who had every reason to resent the foreign conqueror who had just ended their native royal line, the same priesthood whose temple privileges an occupying power might well have curtailed. A hostile source is not automatically a false one. Some of what Herodotus reports may well be true. But a source with this much plausible motive to portray a conqueror badly deserves exactly the same skepticism this course has applied to sources with a motive to flatter a king, not a free pass simply because its subject happens to be a foreign ruler rather than a beloved one.

📖 CLOSING

Egypt's fall at Pelusium is not, however, the end of this course's story. It closes one chapter, and Cambyses's own reign closes only a few years later, in circumstances this course cannot fully untangle either. What follows is a succession crisis, and a king whose own account of what happened next has, remarkably, survived in stone for this course to read directly. Lesson 2 turns to it now.
