📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can a single carved cliff face unlock an entire lost language, centuries after the empire that carved it is gone?

Cambyses died in 522 BC, on his way back from Egypt, under circumstances no surviving source explains with real confidence. What followed was a genuine succession crisis, and the man who emerged from it, Darius I, left behind an account of his own rise carved directly into a cliff face, in a form this course can still read today.

📚 PART 1 · A CONTESTED THRONE

According to Darius's own inscription, a man claiming to be Cambyses's brother, Bardiya, seized the Persian throne while Cambyses was still in Egypt. Darius's account insists this man was actually an impostor named Gaumata, since the real Bardiya, Darius claims, had secretly been killed by Cambyses's own order years earlier. Darius killed the pretender and took the throne himself, then spent much of his first year as king putting down a wave of rebellions across the empire.

This course should be honest about a real limitation here. Darius's version is the only detailed account of these events to survive, carved at his own command to justify his own rule. No independent Persian or Egyptian record exists to check his claims about Gaumata's true identity against. This does not mean Darius invented the entire episode. It does mean this course cannot simply take a king's own justification for seizing his own throne as neutral, verified fact, any more than it could take the Cyrus Cylinder's language at face value back in Week 17.

📚 PART 2 · A ROCK THAT UNLOCKED AN ENTIRE FIELD

This week's second Primary Source is the inscription itself, known as the Behistun Inscription, carved high into a cliff face at Mount Behistun in what is now western Iran, alongside a large relief image of Darius standing over the defeated pretender and a line of other captured rebel leaders. What makes this inscription genuinely extraordinary is not just its content, but its form: the same text appears three times, carved in three different trilingual cuneiform scripts, Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian, the same script this course has read all semester in the Epic of Gilgamesh, Hammurabi's Code, and Ashurbanipal's library tablets.

Because the same message appeared in three known and unknown scripts side by side, the Behistun Inscription became cuneiform's own key to decipherment, much as the Rosetta Stone had already done for Egyptian hieroglyphics. In the 1830s and 1840s, a British officer and scholar named Henry Rawlinson repeatedly risked his own life scaling the sheer, dangerous cliff face to copy the inscription by hand, section by section. Using the Old Persian portion, already partially understood, as a starting point, Rawlinson and other scholars gradually worked out how to read Babylonian cuneiform as well. This is worth stating plainly, as a closing thought for this entire semester: every cuneiform Primary Source this course has read since Sumer in Week 2 can be read today largely because of the work this single inscription made possible.

📚 PART 3 · ONE EMPIRE, THREE RIVER VALLEYS

With Egypt's conquest in this week's opening lesson and Darius's throne now secure, this course has reached a genuine turning point. For the first time in this semester's story, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Persia's own homeland all answer to one empire, an achievement no earlier power this course has studied, not Sargon, not Hammurabi, not Assyria at its height, ever managed. And this empire does not stop looking outward. The Ionian Greek cities along Anatolia's coast, this course already knows from Weeks 16 and 17, have been Persian subjects since Cyrus's conquest of Lydia. Just across the Aegean Sea lie the mainland Greek city-states this course has not yet studied in any depth at all.

This is where the Fall Semester's story ends, and deliberately so: one vast empire, newly unified across three river valleys, now looking west toward a scattered handful of small, quarrelsome Greek city-states that, at this exact moment, would seem to have no realistic chance against it at all. What actually comes next for Greece, and for Persia's own ambitions in that direction, is a story for another semester.

📖 CLOSING

Eighteen weeks ago, this course began with small Neolithic farming villages. It closes this semester with three great river valleys, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Persia's own homeland, bound under a single ruler, and a rock cliff in the Zagros foothills that quietly made every cuneiform source along the way readable in the first place. This week's Discussion Topic and capstone VR activity ask exactly the question this closing thought raises: with one empire now this large, what comes next for Greece?
