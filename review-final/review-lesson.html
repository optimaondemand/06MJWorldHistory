📖 LOOKING BACK ACROSS EIGHTEEN WEEKS

This course began with a question so basic it is easy to forget anyone ever had to answer it for the first time: why farm at all? Eighteen weeks later, that question has led through the world's first cities, its first empires, a genuine collapse, and a new empire wide enough to hold three river valleys at once. Before this course closes for the semester, it is worth standing back and looking at the whole shape of that story together.

📚 PART 1 · FARMING, CITIES, AND THE FIRST KINGS

The story opened at Jericho and Çatalhöyük, where families first committed themselves to fields instead of following herds and wild grain across the seasons, a choice that, this course was honest to admit, did not obviously make life easier at first. That commitment produced surplus, and surplus produced Sumer's walled cities, above all Uruk, the first true city this course studied. Cities needed record-keepers, and record-keepers needed cuneiform, the wedge-shaped script this course has now read across four thousand years of Mesopotamian history, from the Sumerian King List to Ashurbanipal's library to the Behistun Inscription itself.

Out of Sumer's rival cities rose the world's first empire, built by Sargon of Akkad, a man legend says began his life set adrift on a river in a basket of reeds. It is worth pausing on that basket, because it is a small, fragile thing to have an empire depend on. Had it sunk, there is no obvious reason another Sargon would have appeared to unite Mesopotamia's cities when he did, or in the way he did. Empires, this course has argued all semester, are not owed to history. A century or two later, Hammurabi of Babylon offered a different kind of answer to the same problem Sargon faced, binding a kingdom together not chiefly through conquest but through a public code of law, one of this course's clearest tests of the idea that stable offices and predictable justice are what let a large community actually hold together.

📚 PART 2 · EGYPT, AND THE WORLD BEYOND THE TWO RIVERS

While Mesopotamia's kings rose and fell, Egypt built something unusually durable under one crown, first joined by Narmer, then expressed in the Old Kingdom's pyramids, and later carried forward by rulers like Hatshepsut and Thutmose III, who pursued Egypt's greatness by two different roads, one through trade and diplomacy, one through conquest, without either approach proving simply superior to the other. This course also stepped briefly outside its main Near Eastern and Egyptian story to meet the planned streets of Mohenjo-daro and the oracle bones of Shang China, a reminder that this semester's deep story was never the only ancient story worth telling, only the one this course had chosen to follow most closely.

📚 PART 3 · A BRONZE WORLD, AND ITS COLLAPSE

By the time this course reached Crete's palaces, Mycenaean Greece, and the Hittite Empire, an interconnected Bronze Age world had formed, bound together by trade, marriage alliances, and treaties like the one Egypt and Hatti signed after Kadesh. Then, within little more than a lifetime, it came apart. This course was careful not to hand that collapse a single cause, weighing drought, internal unrest, and disrupted trade together rather than pointing to any one dramatic villain. It is worth asking, as this course did in Week 9, what might have happened had the disruptions historians call the Sea Peoples arrived two centuries earlier or later than they did. A world already weakened by other pressures met them when it did. A stronger world, or one caught at a different moment, might have absorbed the shock the way Egypt, battered but intact, eventually did.

📚 PART 4 · FILLING THE VACUUM

Out of that collapse's wreckage rose the powers of Quarter 2: Kushite kings ruling Egypt from the south, Assyria's iron-armed professional soldiers, Ashurbanipal's library and Nineveh's eventual fall in 612 BC, Nebuchadnezzar's Babylon and its fall of Jerusalem, and the patient, centuries-long rise of the Medes. Each of these powers solved the same first-order problems in a different way, terror in Assyria's case, splendor and law in Babylon's, slow endurance in Media's, and each carried both real strength and a real cost.

📖 PART 5 · ONE EMPIRE, AT LAST

That is the arc this course closes on: Cyrus the Great, another figure legend places in danger as an infant, uniting Media and Persia, then Lydia, then Babylon itself, largely by offering conquered peoples less punishment than Assyria or Babylon had. His son Cambyses finished the work at Pelusium, and Darius left his own account carved into a cliff at Behistun, in three languages at once, the very inscription that let modern scholars read everything this course has read all semester. For the first time, Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Persia's own homeland answered to one ruler. That empire does not stop there. It already rules Greek cities along the Anatolian coast, and just across the Aegean sit the small, quarrelsome city-states of mainland Greece. What happens next between them is a story for another semester.
