📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
What if Hammurabi had died at fifty?

The Akkadian Empire's collapse, which closed last week's lesson, left a power vacuum across Mesopotamia. No single city held the kind of dominance Sargon or Naram-Sin once claimed. Into this unsettled landscape, over the following centuries, came waves of a semi-nomadic, Semitic-speaking people the Mesopotamians called the Amorites, migrating in from the western desert fringes and the Levant beginning around 2000 to 1900 BC. Amorite chieftains and their followers gradually settled among the existing Mesopotamian cities, and in some cases, simply took them over, establishing new ruling dynasties while largely adopting the language, writing system, and religious practices already in place. This is worth noting carefully: the Amorites did not erase Mesopotamian civilization when they arrived. They stepped into it, adapted to it, and eventually became some of its most consequential rulers.

📚 PART 1 · A MINOR CITY RISES

Among the many small city-states an Amorite dynasty came to rule was Babylon, a relatively minor town on the Euphrates with no particular claim to greatness at the start of the second millennium BC. That changed with the reign of Hammurabi, who took Babylon's throne around 1792 BC. For much of his reign, Hammurabi ruled a modest kingdom, cautious and largely defensive, forming and breaking alliances with larger neighboring powers as circumstances demanded. Only late in his reign, in a rapid series of campaigns during his final decade of rule, did Hammurabi complete the military unification of southern and much of central Mesopotamia under Babylon's rule, defeating rival cities including Larsa, Mari, and Eshnunna.

This timing matters. It would be easy to imagine Hammurabi as a conqueror from the very start of his reign, methodically building an empire year by year. The actual pattern was different: decades of comparatively quiet, defensive kingship, followed by a late, decisive burst of conquest once the right combination of weakened rivals and opportunity appeared. Contingency shows up here too, just as it did with Sargon in Week 3: had Hammurabi died at fifty instead of ruling into his final campaigning years, Babylon might have remained a middling kingdom, remembered, if at all, as one Amorite city-state among many rather than the namesake of an age.

📚 PART 2 · MORE THAN A CONQUEROR

What makes Hammurabi's reign matter to history far beyond his military campaigns is what he is best remembered for today: a written law code, inscribed on a monumental stone stele and set up publicly for his subjects to see. This was not the first law code in Mesopotamian history; earlier Sumerian rulers had issued their own, less complete legal collections. But Hammurabi's Code survives largely intact, is remarkably comprehensive across nearly every area of daily life, family, property, labor, agriculture, trade, and it explicitly frames Hammurabi's kingship around the promise of justice for his subjects, a claim Lesson 2 examines in detail.

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW · A NOTE ON CHRONOLOGY
Historians place Hammurabi's reign at roughly 1792 to 1750 BC using what is called the "Middle Chronology," one of several competing systems scholars use to anchor ancient Near Eastern dates, each shifting Hammurabi's reign by a few decades earlier or later depending on which astronomical and textual evidence is given priority. This course uses the Middle Chronology consistently, but it is worth remembering that even a king as famous as Hammurabi sits inside a timeline that professional historians still actively debate and refine.
