📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Can a law code be both fair and unfair at once?

Hammurabi had a stele, a tall stone monument, carved with 282 laws and set up in a temple where his subjects could see it, or have it read to them. This week's primary sources let you read a selection of those laws directly, along with the code's prologue, in which Hammurabi describes his own purpose. This lesson gives you the tools to evaluate what you'll read there.

📚 PART 1 · JUDGING A REGIME BY WHAT IT SOLVES

One useful way historians evaluate an ancient government is to ask a very practical question: does it solve the first-order problems every political community faces? Does it establish stable offices whose authority people actually recognize? Does it administer common laws regularly and predictably, rather than leaving justice to the whims of whoever happens to be powerful in a given dispute? Does it give people a peaceful way to resolve conflicts with each other, rather than forcing every wronged family to take revenge on its own? Judged by these standards, Hammurabi's Code is a genuine achievement. It covers an enormous range of ordinary life: contracts, wages, marriage, inheritance, the responsibilities of doctors, builders, and boat-builders, the penalties for theft and fraud, and the standards owed between landlord and tenant. A merchant, a farmer, or a family with a grievance had, at least in principle, a public, written standard to appeal to, rather than only the private judgment of a local strongman.

📚 PART 2 · "AN EYE FOR AN EYE": PROPORTIONAL JUSTICE

The Code's most famous principle is lex talionis, Latin for "law of retaliation": punishment should match the harm done, an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth, a broken bone for a broken bone. Read against the backdrop of what came before it, feuds and revenge killings that could spiral out of all proportion to the original wrong, lex talionis represents a genuine limiting principle: the law caps what a wronged party is entitled to take in return, rather than leaving retaliation open-ended. That is a real form of justice, even if it looks harsh by modern standards.

At the same time, a close reading of the Code reveals that its justice was not equally applied to everyone. Babylonian society, as the Code assumes throughout, was organized into distinct classes: the amelu, free persons of high or full legal status; the mushkenum, a class of free but dependent commoners with fewer legal protections and different (often lower) payments owed to or by them; and slaves, who held property status under the law rather than full legal personhood. The same injury, an eye put out, a bone broken, carried a different penalty depending on the social class of the victim and the offender. This is worth sitting with honestly rather than smoothing over: Hammurabi's Code offered real, predictable, publicly known justice, and at the same time built inequality directly into its structure. Both things are true simultaneously, and a fair evaluation of the Code has to hold both.

🎯 TRY IT: PENALTY BY CLASS
Under Hammurabi's Code, a full injury penalty (an eye for an eye) applied between two amelu. For an injury to a person of lower legal status, what did the Code substitute instead?
An amelu puts out the eye of a mushkenum (a free but dependent commoner).
An amelu breaks the bone of another man's slave.
⚠️ DON'T FLATTEN THIS
It is tempting to call the Code either "just" or "unjust" and stop there. Resist that. The Code genuinely limited revenge and gave people a public standard to appeal to, and it genuinely treated people unequally by class. A fair evaluation holds both facts at once rather than picking the one that is easier to say.
📚 PART 3 · NUMBERS, PRECISELY KEPT

Notice, as you read this week's laws, how often the Code specifies exact quantities: so many shekels of silver, such-and-such a fraction of a harvest, precise wage schedules for different trades. This reflects something distinctive about Babylonian intellectual life: a highly developed mathematics built on a sexagesimal, or base-60, number system (the same system that still gives us 60 minutes in an hour and 360 degrees in a circle). Babylonian scribes trained in this system could perform sophisticated calculations, including the geometric relationships preserved on a famous clay tablet known as Plimpton 322, which lists sets of numbers matching what we now call Pythagorean triples, whole numbers satisfying the Pythagorean theorem, over a thousand years before Pythagoras himself was born in Greece. A legal system this precise about numbers and a mathematical tradition this sophisticated developed side by side, in the same scribal schools, and neither should be studied in isolation from the other.

📚 PART 4 · KINGSHIP JUSTIFIED BY JUSTICE

Finally, notice how Hammurabi frames his own right to rule. He does not simply claim power by force of arms. In the Code's prologue, which you will read this week, Hammurabi presents himself as chosen by the gods specifically to "cause justice to prevail in the land," to protect the weak from the strong. Whether or not Hammurabi's courts always lived up to that promise in daily practice is a separate question from whether the promise itself mattered. A king who justifies his rule by appeal to justice, rather than by naked force alone, has set a standard his own subjects, and history, can measure him against.
