💡 ABOUT THIS SOURCE
This is an adapted classroom account, based on the well-documented findings of Kathleen Kenyon's excavations at Tell es-Sultan (ancient Jericho), carried out between 1952 and 1958. It is not a verbatim quotation from Kenyon's own published reports; it is a careful summary, in report form, of what her team actually found and how they interpreted it, written so you can practice reading an archaeological account directly.
📖 THE ACCOUNT

Our trench cut deep into the eastern side of the tell, through more than twenty feet of accumulated debris, before we reached a layer that surprised every member of the team. At this depth, we uncovered a freestanding stone wall, still standing to a height of several feet in places, built of rough, unworked stones set without mortar. Running alongside it, cut into the bedrock itself, lay a wide, deep ditch. Neither the wall nor the ditch could have been the work of a small or disorganized group; moving this much stone and cutting this much rock by hand would have required sustained, coordinated labor over a considerable stretch of time.

Attached to the inner face of the wall, we found something more remarkable still: a solid stone tower, circular in plan, standing over twenty-six feet high and roughly thirty feet across at its base. An internal stairway, its stone steps still intact, climbs through the heart of the tower from bottom to top. We know of no other structure like it anywhere in the world from so early a period. What is most striking is what we did not find in these same layers: no fragments of fired pottery anywhere in the deposits associated with the wall and tower. Whoever built this place did so before the invention of pottery, relying instead on baskets, skins, and unfired clay vessels for their household needs.

We cannot yet say with certainty why the people of this settlement built a wall, a ditch, and a tower on this scale. A defensive purpose against raiders or rival groups is the most straightforward explanation, and it may well be correct. But we have also considered whether the wall might have served to hold back seasonal flooding or debris flow from the hills above the spring at the site's edge, or whether the tower carried some meaning beyond the practical, a mark of the community's identity or its claim to this place. What the excavation makes clear beyond doubt is this: many centuries before anyone in this region wrote a word, worked metal, or made a pot, the people who lived here were already capable of large, coordinated construction projects, and were committed enough to this one spot of ground to build something meant to last.

Beneath and around these structures, we recovered the remains of round houses built of shaped mudbrick, evidence of a settled population large enough, and organized enough, to undertake defensive or monumental works well before any of the technologies we usually associate with early civilization had been invented.
