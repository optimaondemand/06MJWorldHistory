📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Was farming really an obvious improvement over foraging?

Ten thousand years ago, nearly every human being on Earth lived the same way. Small bands of families moved with the seasons, following herds of wild animals and the ripening of wild plants. They knew their territory intimately: which valley held ripe wild wheat in early summer, which hillside sheltered ibex in winter. This was not a poor way to live. Studies of the few hunter-gatherer societies that survived into modern times suggest that many foragers ate a varied diet, worked fewer hours than farmers would later work, and suffered less from certain diseases. Keep that in mind as this lesson unfolds, because it is tempting to assume that farming was simply a better idea that someone finally had. The truth is stranger and slower than that.

📚 PART 1 · THE FERTILE CRESCENT

The story of farming begins in a curved band of hill country stretching from the eastern Mediterranean coast, up through what is now Turkey, and down into the valleys of Mesopotamia. Geographers call this region the Fertile Crescent. It is not, for the most part, a well-watered plain; much of it is rocky highland. But it happened to hold wild ancestors of wheat, barley, lentils, and chickpeas, along with wild sheep, goats, cattle, and pigs, all living in the same general area. Nowhere else on Earth did so many useful wild species overlap so conveniently.

Beginning around 9500 BC, people living in this region started doing something new: instead of only gathering wild grain, they began deliberately planting seeds from the plants with the traits they liked best, larger seeds, tougher stalks that didn't shatter and scatter grain before harvest. Over many centuries of this selective replanting, wild wheat and barley slowly changed into true domesticated crops. By around 8500 BC, domesticated einkorn wheat, emmer wheat, and barley were established across the region. Sheep and goats followed close behind, domesticated by roughly 8000 BC, with cattle and pigs not far after. This whole shift, from gathering and hunting wild food to deliberately growing and herding it, is called the Neolithic Revolution. Note that "revolution" here does not mean it happened quickly. It unfolded over dozens of generations, and in most places, foraging and early farming existed side by side for a very long time before farming won out.

📚 PART 2 · A REAL TRADE-OFF, NOT AN OBVIOUS UPGRADE

Why does this matter for how you think about history? Because it would be easy, looking back with the advantage of knowing how the story turns out, to assume farming was an obvious improvement that any sensible person would choose. The archaeological evidence complicates that story. Skeletons from early farming villages often show signs of harder physical labor, more tooth decay from a grain-heavy diet, and more evidence of infectious disease, likely worsened by living in permanent settlements alongside domesticated animals and their waste. A forager who fell ill could move away from where sickness had spread. A farmer, tied to fields that took a full growing season to produce a harvest, could not simply walk away.

So why did farming spread anyway? Part of the answer is that farming produces something foraging cannot reliably produce: surplus, more food than a family needs to survive right now. Surplus changes everything downstream. A family with surplus grain can support a member who does not spend all day gathering food, a potter, a builder, eventually a priest or a soldier. Surplus stored in granaries can carry a community through a bad season that would have scattered a foraging band. And a settled, farming population without the need to constantly relocate tends to have more children survive to adulthood, since infants no longer have to be carried on long seasonal migrations. Over enough generations, farming populations simply outgrew foraging ones in the same territory, whatever the individual trade-offs in daily life. This is sedentism, the practice of staying in one place year-round, and it is the quiet engine underneath everything that follows in this course: villages, cities, kings, writing, and armies all depend on people staying put long enough to build them.

💡 HELPFUL TO KNOW · LEARNING TO READ DEEP TIME
A decade is ten years, a century is one hundred years, and a millennium is one thousand years. This course uses BC ("before Christ," sometimes written BCE) for dates before year 1, and AD ("anno Domini") for dates after. One tricky feature of BC dates: they count downward toward the present, so 9500 BC is older than 8500 BC, even though 9500 is the larger number. You will need this vocabulary every week from here forward.
