📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do we know any of this happened?

How do we know any of what Lesson 1 described? No one alive today watched wild wheat become domesticated wheat. The answer is archaeology, the careful study of the physical remains people leave behind: buildings, tools, seeds, bones, and trash. Archaeologists don't simply dig for treasure. They perform an excavation, a slow, layer-by-layer removal of soil, recording exactly where every object was found and at what depth, because the depth of a layer usually tells you its age relative to the layers above and below it. When people build, abandon, and rebuild on the same spot for thousands of years, the site grows upward into a mound called a tell. Walk across the Middle East today and you will see dozens of these grassy or dusty hills, each one a stack of ancient towns waiting to be read like the pages of a book, oldest page on the bottom.

📚 PART 1 · JERICHO: WALLS BEFORE POTTERY

One of the most remarkable tells anywhere is at Jericho, near the Jordan River in the Levant. In the early 1950s, the British archaeologist Kathleen Kenyon excavated deep into Jericho's mound and found something startling: a massive stone tower, over eight meters tall, attached to a substantial stone wall, dating to roughly 8000 BC. This made Jericho one of the very oldest walled settlements ever discovered, built by people who had not yet even invented pottery.

Why build a monumental tower and wall so early, before writing, before the wheel, before fired clay pots? Kenyon herself debated the answer. A defensive wall against raiders is the obvious guess, but some archaeologists have argued the wall might have held back flash floods or seasonal mudslides instead, or that the tower served some ceremonial or status purpose we don't fully understand. This is worth sitting with honestly: sometimes the physical evidence is clear, but the human motive behind it is not. That gap between what we can prove and what we can only guess is part of what makes history a discipline of careful reasoning, not simple fact-retrieval.

📚 PART 2 · ÇATALHÖYÜK: A TOWN WITHOUT STREETS

Roughly a thousand years later and several hundred kilometers north, in what is now central Turkey, a very different kind of Neolithic town grew up at Çatalhöyük. Excavated beginning in the 1960s by James Mellaart, Çatalhöyük turns out to have had no streets at all. Its mudbrick houses were built wall-to-wall in a dense honeycomb, and residents entered their homes not through a front door but down a ladder through a hole in the roof, walking across the rooftops of the whole settlement to get from house to house.

Inside many homes, Mellaart's team found plastered walls decorated with paintings of hunting scenes, and startling installations of bucrania, the horned skulls of wild cattle (aurochs), set into walls and platforms, evidence of ritual or ancestral significance that archaeologists still debate the exact meaning of.

⚠️ DON'T MIX THESE UP · WHAT A REPORT CAN AND CANNOT TELL YOU
Kenyon's and Mellaart's excavation reports are primary sources, but only about the dig itself. What Kenyon wrote is a firsthand, eyewitness account of what her team actually found in the ground at Jericho in the 1950s; that much is primary evidence, the direct testimony of someone who was there. But neither Kenyon nor Mellaart lived in the Neolithic period they were excavating. Their reports are secondary evidence about the Neolithic itself, careful, expert interpretation built from physical clues, not eyewitness memory of Neolithic life. Learning to ask "is this person a firsthand witness to the event I actually care about, or an expert interpreting evidence about it afterward?" will serve you all year.
📚 PART 3 · PLACING THESE SITES ON THE MAP

Jericho sits at roughly 31.9 degrees north latitude and 35.4 degrees east longitude, near the Jordan River valley. Çatalhöyük sits much farther north and slightly west, at roughly 37.7 degrees north latitude and 32.8 degrees east longitude, on the Anatolian plateau. Take a moment with a world map or atlas to plot both points.

Notice how far apart they are, and yet how similar their basic story is: small bands of people, in two unconnected corners of the Fertile Crescent's wider neighborhood, independently committing to permanent settlement within a thousand years of each other. That is not a coincidence of two clever individuals; it is what happens when the deeper shift described in Lesson 1, the slow domestication of plants and animals, reaches the point where staying in one place finally makes more sense than moving. This week's primary sources let you look directly at what archaeologists actually found at both places.
